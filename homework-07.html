Three conjugate Bayes estimators: update, then average

Assignment 7 asks for the Bayes estimator under squared-error loss in three conjugate models. The answer is the posterior mean each time, and each posterior mean is a weighted average of the sample mean and the prior mean, with weights set by how much the prior is worth in observations. The three laboratories below run each update live: steer the prior and the data and watch the posterior—and the estimate—negotiate between them.

Problem 1 · Normal data, normal prior

Precisions add, means average

Data \(N(\theta,\sigma^2)\) with \(\sigma^2\) known; prior \(\theta\sim N(\eta,\tau^2)\). The posterior is normal with precision \(1/\tau^2+n/\sigma^2\), and the Bayes estimator is \( \delta = \frac{n\tau^2}{n\tau^2+\sigma^2}\,\bar y + \frac{\sigma^2}{n\tau^2+\sigma^2}\,\eta \).

prior likelihood of ȳ posterior

Grow \(n\) and the posterior chases the likelihood; tighten \(\tau\) and it clings to the prior. The weight on the data is the data's share of the total precision—the same arithmetic that ran through Assignments 3 and 5.

Problem 2 · Bernoulli data, Beta prior

Successes join α, failures join β

Prior Beta\((\alpha,\beta)\), data \(s\) successes in \(n\) trials. Posterior Beta\((\alpha+s,\;\beta+n-s)\); Bayes estimator \( \delta=\frac{\alpha+s}{\alpha+\beta+n} \), a compromise between \(\bar y = s/n\) and the prior mean \(\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)\) in which the prior counts for \(\alpha+\beta\) pseudo-observations.

prior posterior prior mean · δ · ȳ

Set \(\alpha=\beta=1\): the uniform prior, and the estimator becomes \((1+s)/(2+n)\)—the shaded estimator this course has carried since Assignment 1, Laplace's rule of succession.

Problem 3 · Poisson data, Gamma prior

Counts join the shape, exposure joins the rate

Prior Gamma\((\alpha,\beta)\) in the shape–rate convention (mean \(\alpha/\beta\)); data a Poisson sample with total \(s\). Posterior Gamma\((\alpha+s,\;\beta+n)\); Bayes estimator \( \delta=\frac{\alpha+s}{\beta+n} \): the prior is worth \(\beta\) observations of exposure carrying \(\alpha\) events.

prior posterior prior mean · δ · ȳ

All three problems share one skeleton: \( \delta = w\,\bar y + (1-w)\times\text{prior mean} \) with \( w = n/(n+\text{prior weight}) \). The prior weight is \(\sigma^2/\tau^2\) observations here, \(\alpha+\beta\) trials in Problem 2, \(\beta\) exposure in Problem 3—and in every case \(w\to1\): the data buy out the prior.
