Exercises 5–6 · The minimax binomial estimator

A risk that does not depend on \(p\)

The estimator \( d'=(X+\sqrt n/2)/(n+\sqrt n) \) pulls the observed rate \(X/n\) toward \(\tfrac12\). Its risk is the flat line \( \Rs(p,d')=1/[4(\sqrt n+1)^2] \) (Exercise 6)—the same at every \(p\), which is what makes it minimax. The plain rate \(X/n\) has risk \( p(1-p)/n \), a hump that is smaller at the extremes but larger in the middle. Slide \(n\) and watch the two cross.

rate \(X/n\): risk \(p(1-p)/n\) minimax \(d'\): risk \(1/[4(\sqrt n+1)^2]\)

The minimax rule trades a worse risk near \(p=\tfrac12\) for a better one than \(X/n\) would have at its own worst case: its flat height \(1/[4(\sqrt n+1)^2]\) sits just below the rate's peak \(1/(4n)\). As \(n\) grows the hump flattens toward the line and the advantage fades—minimaxity is a small-sample insurance policy.

Exercise 7 · Inverse sampling

The unbiased estimator is not the obvious one

Fish are caught until \(n\) of a type appear; \(X\) is the total caught, negative binomial. The tempting estimator \(n/X\) is biased upward; the fix is \((n-1)/(X-1)\), which is exactly unbiased for \(p\). The curves below plot the expected value of each as \(p\) varies—one rides the diagonal \( \E=p \), the other floats above it. Slide \(n\).

\((n-1)/(X-1)\): unbiased, \(\E=p\) \(n/X\): biased upward

The upward bias of \(n/X\) is Jensen's inequality: \(x\mapsto n/x\) is convex, so \( \E\{n/X\}>n/\E\{X\}=p \). Lowering both the numerator and the count by one is precisely the correction that lands the expectation on \(p\). The gap is largest for small \(n\) and shrinks as the sample of successes grows.

Exercise 29 · The best multiple of s²

What unbiasedness costs

The known-mean variance estimator \(s^2\) is unbiased for \(\sigma^2\) (Exercise 14). But among all multiples \(ks^2\), the expected squared error \( E(ks^2-\sigma^2)^2 = \sigma^4\big[k^2(n+2)/n - 2k + 1\big] \) is minimized not at \(k=1\) but at \( k = n/(n+2) \): shrinking beats unbiased, at every \(\sigma^2\) and every \(n\). Slide \(n\) and hover the curve.

\(E(ks^2-\sigma^2)^2/\sigma^4\) minimizer \(k=n/(n+2)\) unbiased \(k=1\)

As \(n\) grows the two dots merge and the argument fades—the unbiasedness penalty is a small-sample phenomenon. At small \(n\) it is not small: at \(n=2\) the shrunken estimator's expected squared error is half the unbiased one's.

Exercises 33–34 · Estimating a binomial's number of trials

A moment estimator that wobbles

From \(m\) counts of heads in eight tosses (true \(n=8\), \(p=\tfrac12\)), the method of moments gives \( \hat n = m_1^2/(m_1-\hat s^2) \). Because it divides by the small gap between the sample mean and variance, ordinary noise throws it a long way. The histogram shows \(\hat n\) across 400 replications; slide \(m\) to watch the spread tighten—slowly.

\(\hat n\) across 400 replications true \(n=8\)

Even the median \(\hat n\) drifts and the spread is wide until \(m\) runs into the hundreds; the tail of large \(\hat n\) is the small-denominator blow-up. Some replications give \(\hat s^2\ge m_1\) and no admissible \(\hat n\) at all—those are dropped and counted below. Estimating how many trials produced a set of counts is a genuinely hard problem, and the moment estimator is not equal to it at small \(m\).

Exercises 42–45 · The interval for a variance

A pivot that is not symmetric

With \(\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2/\sigma^2\sim\chi^2_{\nu}\), a confidence interval for \(\sigma^2\) brackets the chi-square between two percentiles and inverts. The chi-square is skewed, so the two cut points sit at unequal distances and the resulting interval \(\big(\mathrm{SS}/\chi^2_{\text{hi}},\ \mathrm{SS}/\chi^2_{\text{lo}}\big)\) reaches farther above the point estimate than below. Slide the degrees of freedom, the confidence level, and the sum of squares.

\(\chi^2_{\nu}\) density the two percentiles

The shaded central mass is the confidence level; the unequal tails are the skew. Notice the interval's upper limit runs well past twice the lower for small \(\nu\)—a variance is pinned down loosely until the sample is large, which is why it demands more data than a mean for the same relative precision.

Exercise 53 · A posterior shifted to the data

The likelihood cancels the prior

A uniform observation on \([0,\theta]\) with the Gamma\((2,1)\) prior \(\pi(\theta)=\theta e^{-\theta}\): the joint collapses to \(e^{-\theta}\), the marginal is \(e^{-x}\), and the posterior is a unit exponential shifted to start at the observed \(x\), \( f(\theta\mid x)=e^{-(\theta-x)} \) for \(\theta>x\). Its mean is \( E(\theta\mid x)=x+1 \). Slide the observation and watch the whole posterior slide with it.

prior \(\theta e^{-\theta}\) posterior \(e^{-(\theta-x)},\ \theta>x\) Bayes estimate \(x+1\)

The observation supplies a hard floor—\(\theta\) cannot be below the value already seen—and the prior adds exactly one expected unit above it. As \(x\) grows the posterior marches right, never doubling back below \(x\), and the estimate tracks it at a constant lead of one.

Exercises 59–60 · Best predictor versus best linear predictor

When the line is exact, and when it only approximates

The best predictor is \(E(Y\mid x)\); the best linear predictor of Formula (31) is the least-squares line through the joint distribution. For \(f=8xy\) (Exercise 59) the conditional mean is already a line, so the two coincide exactly. For \(f=(2x+4y)/3\) (Exercise 60) the conditional mean is a gentle curve and the line only approximates it. Toggle the joint density.

best predictor \(E(Y\mid x)\) best linear predictor \(\ell(x)\)

Restricting to lines loses nothing in Exercise 59—the curves lie on top of one another—and almost nothing in Exercise 60, where they run within about \(0.01\) of each other across the whole range. The best linear predictor is the cheapest useful summary of a dependence: two numbers, a slope and an intercept, standing in for a whole conditional mean.
