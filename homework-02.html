Conditional expectation, the two ways to miss, and what the sample size does to a shrinkage estimator

Assignment 2 rebuilds conditional expectation and the decision-theoretic vocabulary, then compares two estimators of a binomial probability: the observed rate \(d_0=Y/n\) and the shrunken \(d_1=Y/(n+c)\). The write-up derives the risk functions \(R_{d_0}(p)=p(1-p)/n\) and \(R_{d_1}(p)=\bigl(np(1-p)+c^2p^2\bigr)/(n+c)^2\) and shows their ratio tends to one. Problems 4–6 · Conditional expectation

A number and a random variable

For a standardized bivariate normal pair with correlation \(\rho\), the conditional distribution of \(Y\) given \(X=x_0\) is the renormalized slice of the joint at \(x_0\), and its mean is \( \E\{Y\mid X=x_0\}=\rho x_0 \)—one number. Collect those numbers over every \(x\) and you have the line \(x\mapsto\rho x\); the random variable \( \E\{Y\mid X\}=\rho X \) is that line evaluated wherever \(X\) lands.

the function \(x\mapsto \E\{Y\mid X=x\}\) conditional density of \(Y\) at \(x_0\) (drawn sideways) \(\E\{Y\mid X=x_0\}\)

Slide \(x_0\): the dot walks the line—that is the random variable in action, its value crystallizing once \(X\) does. Slide \(\rho\): the whole prediction rule changes. Averaging the line's height over the distribution of \(X\) gives \(\E\{\E\{Y\mid X\}\}=\rho\,\E\{X\}=0=\E\{Y\}\): iterated expectations, visible.

Problem 12 · The bias–variance decomposition

The two ways to miss

Under squared-error loss an estimator pays for exactly two defects: $$ \E^{\theta}\{(\delta-g(\theta))^2\} \;=\; \operatorname{Var}^{\theta}(\delta) + b(\theta)^2 . $$ Move the estimator's spread and its systematic offset and watch the mean squared error re-apportion—same currency, two sins.

MSE, decomposed

An unbiased estimator lives at \(b=0\), where risk is all variance—the case the course text uses to compare unbiased estimators by variance alone. A little bias is worth buying whenever it comes with a large enough variance reduction; the next widget prices exactly that trade.

Problems 15–16 · Risk functions and the limit

Shrinkage against sample size

The two rules for \(Y\sim\text{Binomial}(n,p)\) under \(L(p,a)=(p-a)^2\): the unbiased rate \(d_0=Y/n\) and the shrunken \(d_1=Y/(n+c)\), which pulls toward \(0\). Their risk curves cross at \( p^{*}(n)=\dfrac{2n+c}{(c+2)n+c} \), so neither dominates at any sample size—but the stakes collapse as \(n\) grows: the bias² that distinguishes \(d_1\) is \(O(1/n^2)\) against variances of \(O(1/n)\). Small \(n\): the curves separate and the choice of estimator is a real decision. Large \(n\): the data drown the shrinkage and the ratio (right) flattens onto \(1\)—the limit of Problem 16, arriving in real time. Keeping the trade alive forever requires \(c\propto\sqrt{n}\), which is precisely how the course text's minimax estimator is built.
