Homework 10 — Interactive Companion

The two-group normal model, two ways: the fit that is a pair of group means, the variance estimate that runs low, the standard error of the treatment effect, and the t that studentizes it

Assignment 10 carries the two-group model worked in lecture—a control group and a treated group of normal observations with a common variance—through eleven parts, twice over: once with the group means \(a_1,a_2\) as the parameters, and once in the lecture's regression coding \(b_0=a_1,\ b_1=a_2-a_1\), with \(b_1\) the treatment effect. The write-up does the algebra; the four laboratories below put the moving parts on screen. The maximum likelihood fit is nothing but the pair of group averages, read two ways (Parts 1–4). The variance MLE divides by \(n\) and lands systematically below \(\sigma^2\) (Part 6). The treatment effect's standard error is \(\sigma\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}\), smallest when the two groups are the same size (Parts 8&10). And the studentized coefficient \((\hat b_1-b_1)/\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}\) follows a \(t\) on \(n-2\) degrees of freedom—fatter in the tails than the normal until the sample grows (Parts 7, 9&11).

Parts 1–4 · The fit, read two ways

Maximum likelihood is the pair of group means

Least squares (equivalently, maximum likelihood under normality) sets each fitted value to its group average: \(\hat a_1=\bar Y_1\), \(\hat a_2=\bar Y_2\). The regression coding reports the same fit differently—\(\hat b_0=\bar Y_1\) is the control level and \(\hat b_1=\bar Y_2-\bar Y_1\) is the vertical jump to the treated level. Slide the true group means and the two sample sizes; the cloud is a fixed set of standard normals shifted into place, so the estimates move smoothly.

control \(Y_{1i}\), mean \(\hat a_1=\hat b_0\) treated \(Y_{2j}\), mean \(\hat a_2\) the jump \(\hat b_1=\hat a_2-\hat a_1\)

The two codings are one fit in two bases: the estimates always satisfy \(\hat b_0=\hat a_1\) and \(\hat b_1=\hat a_2-\hat a_1\), the same invertible relabelling that connects the parameters. The dashed line extends the control mean across the plot; the green segment measures the treatment effect the regression coefficient \(\hat b_1\) reports.

Part 6 · The variance MLE runs low

Dividing by \(n\) instead of \(n-2\)

The MLE \(\hat\sigma^2=\mathrm{RSS}/n\) averages squared residuals about the two fitted means, and residuals about a fitted mean are smaller than errors about the true one—two means were estimated, so \(\mathrm{E}\{\hat\sigma^2\}=\tfrac{n-2}{n}\,\sigma^2<\sigma^2\). The histogram shows \(\hat\sigma^2\) across 600 simulated datasets (true \(\sigma^2=1\)); the unbiased \(s^2=\mathrm{RSS}/(n-2)\) centers on the line instead. Slide the group sizes.

\(\hat\sigma^2=\mathrm{RSS}/n\) across 600 datasets mean of \(\hat\sigma^2\) true \(\sigma^2=1\)

The gap between the two vertical lines is the bias, \(-2\sigma^2/n\): the MLE's mean sits at \((n-2)/n\), the unbiased estimator's at \(1\). It is a small-sample effect—at \(n_1=n_2=2\) the MLE is low by half—and it melts away as the groups grow, but it never quite vanishes. This is why the standard errors in the next lab use \(s\), not \(\hat\sigma\).

Parts 8 & 10 · The standard error of the treatment effect

Why a balanced design wins

The treatment effect's standard error is \(\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat b_1)=s\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}\)—the classical two-sample form, in which estimating a contrast pays the variance of both group means. Hold the total \(N=n_1+n_2\) fixed and split it: the curve is \(\sigma\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}\) as the allocation \(f=n_1/N\) varies, and it bottoms out exactly at the balanced split \(f=\tfrac12\). Slide the total and the allocation.

\(\mathrm{SE}(\hat b_1)=\sigma\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}\) balanced minimum \(f=\tfrac12\) current allocation

The square root makes the penalty forgiving near the middle and punishing at the edges: a 60–40 split costs only about 2% over balanced, but 90–10 nearly doubles the standard error, because the tiny group's mean is barely pinned down and it is the sum \(1/n_1+1/n_2\) that matters. Given a fixed number of subjects, split them evenly to learn the treatment effect most precisely (\(\sigma=1\) is used for the curve).

Parts 7, 9 & 11 · The studentized coefficient is a \(t\)

Fatter tails than the normal, until the sample grows

Standardizing \(\hat b_1\) by its true standard error gives a standard normal; dividing instead by the estimated \(\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}\) replaces \(\sigma\) with \(s\), and because \(\hat b_1\perp s\) (Part 7) the ratio is a standard normal over an independent \(\sqrt{\chi^2_{n-2}/(n-2)}\)—a \(t\) on \(n-2\) degrees of freedom. The histogram is \(T=(\hat b_1-b_1)/\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\hat b_1)\) over 3000 datasets, against the \(t_{n-2}\) density and the \(N(0,1)\) it is often mistaken for. Slide the group sizes.

\(T\) across 3000 datasets \(t_{n-2}\) density \(N(0,1)\) density

The \(t\) matches the simulated statistic where the normal does not: its peak is a touch lower and its tails a touch heavier, so a threshold read off the normal table would reject a true null too often at small \(n\). The degrees of freedom \(n-2\) are exactly the two the variance estimate spent on the two group means; as the groups grow, \(t_{n-2}\to N(0,1)\) and the two curves close up.
