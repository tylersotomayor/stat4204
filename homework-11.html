Homework 11 — Interactive Companion

Simple linear regression, read three ways: the covariance as inverse information, the slope orthogonalized against the intercept, and the slope as a projection onto the centered covariate

Assignment 11 takes the simple linear regression model—an intercept and one covariate, normal errors, and a known common variance—and reads it through the information matrix, the score vector, and the orthogonal geometry of \(n\)-dimensional space, showing the three tell one story. The three laboratories below animate that story: the coefficient covariance \(\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}\) shown to equal the inverse information \(\mathcal I^{-1}\) (Parts 1--2); the slope's estimate written from the scores, its error uncorrelated with the intercept score and unit-correlated with its own (Part 3); and the slope read off as the coefficient of the projection of \(Y\) onto the centered covariate \(\tilde x=x-\bar x\mathbf 1\), the second of three orthogonal subspaces (Parts 4--5).

Parts 1–2 · The covariance is the inverse information

Where the design puts the covariate, and how precisely the slope is known

The estimates have covariance \(\Sigma=\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}\), and the Fisher information is \(\mathcal I=X^\top X/\sigma^2\); the two are exact inverses, \(\Sigma=\mathcal I^{-1}\). Both depend on the design only through the spread of the \(x\)'s: \(\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_1)=\sigma^2/S_{xx}\) shrinks as the covariate spreads out. Slide the \(x\)-spread and the intercept-carrying mean \(\bar x\); the ellipse is the joint confidence set of \((\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1)\), whose shape is \(\Sigma\).

95% set for \((\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1)\), shape \(\Sigma=\mathcal I^{-1}\) truth \((\beta_0,\beta_1)\)

Spreading the \(x\)'s tightens the slope (the ellipse narrows vertically); centering them at \(\bar x=0\) uprights the ellipse, because \(\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1)=-\sigma^2\bar x/S_{xx}\) vanishes there. The tilt you see when \(\bar x\neq0\) is exactly that off-diagonal—the intercept and slope estimates are correlated whenever the covariate is not centered, which is the design-level reason Part 3 must orthogonalize the slope against the intercept.

Part 3 · The slope, written from the scores

Uncorrelated with the intercept score, unit-correlated with its own

Across many simulated data sets the slope error \(\hat\beta_1-\beta_1=\tfrac{\sigma^2}{S_{xx}}(l_1-\bar x\,l_0)\) is plotted against each score: the intercept score \(l_0\propto\sum\varepsilon_i\) (left) and the slope score \(l_1\propto\sum x_i\varepsilon_i\) (right). The theory says the covariances are exactly \(0\) and \(1\)—a flat cloud on the left, a tilted one on the right. Slide the design and toggle which score is on the horizontal axis.

\((\text{score},\ \hat\beta_1-\beta_1)\) across 1500 data sets least-squares fit through the cloud

The cloud against the intercept score has essentially zero slope and \(\widehat{\operatorname{cov}}\approx0\): the slope estimate's error carries no information about the intercept score, no matter how far off-center the covariate sits. Against its own score the covariance sits at \(1\). Under normality uncorrelated means independent, so the slope estimate is genuinely orthogonalized against the intercept—it uses only the part of the data the ones cannot explain.

Parts 4–5 · The slope is a projection onto the centered covariate

\(\hat\beta_1\) is the coordinate of \(Y\) along \(\tilde x=x-\bar x\mathbf 1\)

The least-squares slope is \(\hat\beta_1=\tilde x^\top Y/\tilde x^\top\tilde x\), the coefficient of the projection of \(Y\) onto the line spanned by the centered covariate \(\tilde x=x-\bar x\mathbf 1\)—the residual of \(x\) on the ones, the second of the three orthogonal subspaces \(V_1=\operatorname{span}(\mathbf 1)\), \(V_2=\operatorname{span}(\tilde x)\), \(V_3\). The picture below shows the fit in the \((x,Y)\) plane; slide the true slope and the noise, and watch the fitted line's slope track \(\hat\beta_1\) while the projection onto \(V_2\) carries it.

data \((x_i,Y_i)\) least-squares fit, slope \(\hat\beta_1\) true line, slope \(\beta_1\)

The fit passes through \((\bar x,\bar Y)\)—the intercept subspace \(V_1\) fixes that point—and its slope is the coordinate of \(Y\) along \(\tilde x\). Turning the noise to zero pins \(\hat\beta_1\) exactly on \(\beta_1\); adding noise makes \(\hat\beta_1-\beta_1=\tilde x^\top\varepsilon/S_{xx}\) wander, the whole of it coming from the error's component in \(V_2\). The remaining \(n-2\) dimensions—the subspace \(V_3\)—hold the residuals that, were \(\sigma^2\) unknown, Assignment 10 would have used to estimate it.
