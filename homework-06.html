Maximum likelihood in four models: the likelihood equation, the estimator's spread, and the bound

Assignment 6 takes Assignment 5's four models and asks for the maximum likelihood estimator, its variance, and the comparison to the bound. The answer is the same three times over and then a fourth: the MLE is the sample mean, its variance is exact, and it sits on the bound. The three pieces below show the likelihood equation picking out \(\bar y\), the estimator's sampling distribution against the bound, and the slope-matching picture behind Problem 4.

Problems 1–3 · The likelihood equation

The peak is at the sample mean

Draw a Poisson sample and look at its log likelihood. The peak lands on \(\bar y\) every time—that is the likelihood equation \( s/\theta - n = 0 \) doing its work—and more data make the peak sharper around the true value.

\( \ell(\theta)-\ell(\hat\theta) \) for the drawn sample peak at \(\hat\theta=\bar y\) true θ

The dot is the estimator; the dotted line is the truth. They differ sample to sample, and the gap between them is what the next widget measures.

Problems 1–3 · Variance against the bound

The estimator's spread is the bound

Replicate the experiment: exponential samples with mean \(\theta\), each producing one \(\hat\theta=\bar Y\). The histogram of replications piles up around \(\theta\) with variance \( \theta^2/n \)—which is exactly \( 1/I_n(\theta) \). The running variance readout converges onto the bound as replications accumulate.

histogram of \(\hat\theta\) across replications normal curve with variance \(1/I_n(\theta)=\theta^2/n\)

In these models the match is exact at every \(n\), not just in the limit. That is unusual: in general the reciprocal information describes the MLE's variance only asymptotically.

Problem 4 · The mean parameterization

Match the slope of ψ to the sample mean

In the exponential family \( f(y)\exp[\xi y-\psi(\xi)] \) the likelihood equation says \( \psi'(\hat\xi)=\bar y \): find the point on \(\psi\) whose slope is the sample mean. With \(\theta=\psi'(\xi)\) as the parameter, that point is the estimate, \( \hat\theta=\bar y \). Drawn here for the Poisson case, \(\psi(\xi)=e^{\xi}\). Slide \(\bar y\) and watch the tangency move.

\(\psi(\xi)=e^{\xi}\) tangent with slope \(\bar y\) \(\hat\xi=\log\bar y\), where \(\psi'=\bar y\)

The curvature of \(\psi\) at the tangency is \(\psi''(\hat\xi)\), the one-observation variance—flat stretches of \(\psi\) mean precise estimates of \(\xi\) but imprecise means, and steep curvature the reverse. Assignment 5 found the information \(n\psi''\) in \(\xi\); this assignment finds \(n/\psi''\) in \(\theta\).
