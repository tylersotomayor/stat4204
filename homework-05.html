Fisher information: curvature, the identity between its two forms, and when the bound is attained

Assignment 5 computes seven likelihood quantities—likelihood through the expected second derivative—in four models, and every setting lands on the same pair of identities: the derivative of the log likelihood has mean zero, and its variance equals the negative expected second derivative, the Fisher information \( I_n(\theta) \). The write-up carries the derivations; the three laboratories below let you see the information as curvature, watch the random curvature average into the expected one, and find out where the variance bound is and is not attained.

Problem 1 · Normal, known variance

Information is curvature

For \(n\) i.i.d.\ \(N(\theta,\sigma^2)\) observations the log likelihood is an exact parabola, \( \ell(\theta)-\ell(\bar y) = -\tfrac12 I_n (\theta-\bar y)^2 \) with \( I_n = n/\sigma^2 \): the second derivative is not random, and information is literally the sharpness of the peak. Steer \(n\) and \(\sigma^2\) on a fixed frame.

\( \ell(\theta)-\ell(\bar y) \) at the sliders reference: \(I_n=1\)

More data or less noise sharpen the parabola—the data argue harder against values away from \(\bar y\)—and the two sliders act only through the one number \(n/\sigma^2\): double \(n\) and double \(\sigma^2\) and nothing moves, which is the point. The bound \(1/I_n=\sigma^2/n\) is the variance of \(\bar Y\): attained.

Problems 2–3 · Poisson

Observed curvature, expected information

In the Poisson model the second derivative is random: \( -\ell''(\theta) = S/\theta^2 \) changes with the sample, and only its expectation is the information \( I_n(\theta) = n/\theta \). Draw samples and watch the observed informations scatter around the expected one—and their running mean settle onto it.

observed information \(S/\theta^2\), one dot per sample expected: \(I_n(\theta)=n/\theta\) running mean of the draws

This is the content of the identity \( \operatorname{Var}\{\ell'(\theta)\} = -\mathrm{E}\{\ell''(\theta)\} \): each sample carries its own curvature, and the information is what curvature averages to. In the normal and exponential-family settings of Problems 1 and 4 the dots would all land exactly on the line—there the curvature is a constant.

Problem 3 · The struck-through comparison

Attained for the mean, unattained for the rate

One exponential model, two parameterizations, two verdicts. Estimating the mean \(\theta\), the sample mean attains the bound at every \(n\): \( \operatorname{Var}(\bar Y)/\text{bound} \equiv 1 \). Estimating the rate \(\beta=1/\theta\), the best unbiased estimator \((n-1)/S\) has variance \( \beta^2/(n-2) \) against the bound \( \beta^2/n \): a ratio of \( n/(n-2) \), above one at every finite \(n\) and closing only asymptotically. Both ratios are free of the parameter—the chart is the whole story. Hover to read it.

mean parameterization: \(\bar Y\), ratio \(=1\) rate parameterization: \((n-1)/S\), ratio \(n/(n-2)\)

The bound is attained exactly when the estimator is a linear function of \(\ell'(\theta)\)—for the mean, \( \bar Y-\theta = (\theta^2/n)\,\ell'(\theta) \); for the rate, \((n-1)/S\) is not linear in the score, and no unbiased estimator of \(\beta\) is. Which quantity you estimate decides whether the bound is a target or only a floor.
