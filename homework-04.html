Exercise 3 · Minimax within a pair

The rate against the shaded rule, at any n

Exercise 3 pits \(d_1=x/n\) against \(d_2=(x+1)/(n+2)\) at \(n=2\). Slide \(n\) and watch the whole exercise generalize: the risks cross at \( \theta^{*}=\tfrac12\bigl(1\pm\sqrt{(n+1)/(2n+1)}\bigr) \), the rate's worst case is \(1/4n\) at \(\theta=\tfrac12\), and the shaded rule's worst case sits at the edges for small \(n\)—so the shaded rule is minimax within the pair at every \(n\), while neither rule is ever superior.

\(\mathscr{R}(\theta,d_1)\), the rate \(\mathscr{R}(\theta,d_2)\), shaded suprema · crossings

At \(n=2\) this is the exercise verbatim: worst cases \(\tfrac18\) against \(\tfrac1{16}\), crossings at \((5\mp\sqrt{15})/10\). As \(n\) grows the crossings drift outward and the two curves close on each other—Assignment 2's asymptotics, seen from the minimax side.

Exercises 1 & 4 · Bayes within a class

The linear family for a normal observation

One draw \(X\sim N(\theta,1)\), rules \(d(x)=cx\), risk \( \mathscr{R}(\theta,d)=c^2+(c-1)^2\theta^2 \). Price the family with a uniform prior on \((-w,w)\): the mean risk is \( r(\pi,d)=c^2+(c-1)^2v \) with \(v=w^2/3\), minimized at \(c^{*}=v/(1+v)\). Exercise 4 is the case \(w=1\): \(c^{*}=\tfrac14\), mean risk \(\tfrac14\), against constant risk \(1\) for the unbiased \(c=1\).

\(\mathscr{R}(\theta, cx)\) at the slider's \(c\) \(r(\pi,d)\) as a function of \(c\) the Bayes choice \(c^{*}\)

\(c^{*}=v/(1+v)\) is the prior's share of the total spread—the same precision arithmetic as Assignment 3's \(a^{*}\), shrinking toward the prior mean \(0\). Widen the prior and \(c^{*}\) climbs toward the unbiased \(c=1\); narrow it and the data lose the argument.

Exercises 5 & 6 · A loss that flattens the risk

Same rule, two accountings—and the dial between them

The observed rate \(d(x)=x/n\) has risk \( \theta(1-\theta)/n \) under Exercise 5's plain squared error, and the constant \(1/n\) under Exercise 6's weighted loss \( (a-\theta)^2/\theta(1-\theta) \). The family \( \mathscr{L}_{\gamma}(\theta,a)=(a-\theta)^2/[\theta(1-\theta)]^{\gamma} \) interpolates between them, and under it the rate's risk is \( [\theta(1-\theta)]^{1-\gamma}/n \). The chart plots \(n\times{}\)risk—so the picture depends only on \(\gamma\)—and the slider sweeps Exercise 5 (\(\gamma=0\), the bump) into Exercise 6 (\(\gamma=1\), the equalizer).

\(n\times{}\)risk under \(\mathscr{L}_{\gamma}\) the endpoints: plain bump (\(\gamma=0\)), flat equalizer (\(\gamma=1\))

Only \(\gamma=1\) finishes the job: for any \(\gamma<1\) the risk still dies at the edges, and exactly at \(\gamma=1\) it locks flat. The flattening is engineered: under the fully weighted loss the rate is the Bayes rule for the uniform prior (the weight turns the Beta\((x{+}1,n{-}x{+}1)\) posterior into Beta\((x,n{-}x)\), whose mean is \(x/n\)), and a constant-risk Bayes rule is minimax. Exercise 3's loser becomes optimal by changing what a mistake costs.
