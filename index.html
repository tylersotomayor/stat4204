Notes in Statistical Inference

Based on the Lectures of Daniel Rabinowitz

STAT S4204 · Statistical Inference · Summer 2026

Student notes by Tyler Sotomayor

Lecture Notes

  • Lecture 1From Data to Decisions: Statistical Models, Loss, and RiskMonday–Thursday, 6–9 July 2026OverviewThe course opens where applied statistics leaves off: a study has a goal, data, and software output—and none of those, by themselves, say anything about reality. This lecture builds the bridge. After a review of random variables, distributions, and realizations (and of expectation up through iterated expectations), the data's unknown joint distribution is organized into a statistical model, the study's purpose becomes an analytic goal expressed in the model's parameters, and the choice of method becomes a decision problem: an action space, a loss function, and—as the figure of merit—the risk function, the expected loss as a function of the parameter. Admissibility and the Bayes principle give the two classical answers to what "good" means, and both are put to work on a two-arm clinical trial in which two estimators of a cure-rate difference compete. Thursday's handout then pushes the estimation half one level deeper: restricting attention to unbiased methods, it builds the score and the Fisher information and proves the information bound—a floor under the variance of every unbiased estimator, computed from the model alone and attained exactly by the average of a normal sample. The companion text is Hoel–Port–Stone, Chapter 1 (hps 1); the closing section opens the door to Chapter 2's estimation theory (hps 2).
  • Lecture 2Estimation: Unbiasedness, Maximum Likelihood, and ConfidenceMonday–Thursday, 13–16 July 2026OverviewEstimation gets its own unit: the decision problem in which the action space is the parameter space and the loss is squared error. The week's deck is an itinerary—most of its slides name what is then developed at the board—and the notes work each named item in full. Unbiasedness is defined in its general form and defended as a way of choosing among admissible rules; the information bound is lifted to models with several parameters, where the price of nuisance parameters becomes visible. The unit's centerpiece is the course's first general-purpose constructor of estimators: maximum likelihood, whose estimate sits—approximately, when the information is high—at truth plus score over information, and therefore approximately attains the floor; four classical samples make that exact or nearly so. Confidence sets turn point estimates into precision statements via pivot statistics; conditional expectation returns as the best predictor under squared error; and with a prior on the table, the posterior mean is proved to minimize the Bayes risk, with the normal–normal, binomial–beta, and Poisson–gamma trios worked out. A closing pair of slides reads Bayes methods as shrinkage and penalization, and names the next horizon: sufficiency (hps 2).

Supplementary Material

  • Supplement 1Bayesian Estimation from First PrinciplesCompanion handout · web edition with five live laboratories + typeset PDF · no statistics background assumedOverviewA student-written companion that rebuilds, from nothing, the mathematics behind one sentence from Lecture 2: under squared-error loss, the best estimator is the posterior mean. Every term is defined before it is used, and every derivation shows every algebraic step: why estimation gets stuck without a prior, Bayes' theorem proved by slicing a joint distribution, the best-constant and posterior-mean theorems in full, the Gamma and Beta toolkit, and two complete worked examples with real numbers. The web edition embeds five laboratories where the print edition has figures—slice-and-renormalize Bayes updating, a steerable prior-to-posterior lab, the loss function choosing between mean, median, and mode, and three priors racing one data stream—with the normal–normal update as the centerpiece: its completing-the-square derivation replayed step by step with your numbers, a precision ledger, and a shrinkage strip.

  • Homework 5Fisher Information in Four ModelsAssignment 5 · due Tuesday, 14 July 2026 · interactive page + PDF write-upOverviewThe seven likelihood quantities—likelihood through the expected second derivative—computed in four models: normal with known variance, Poisson, exponential in its mean parameterization, and the natural exponential family f(y)exp[θy − ψ(θ)], whose normalizer generates the moments (mean ψ′, variance ψ″) and unifies the other three by reparameterization.   • Homework 6Maximum Likelihood in Four ModelsAssignment 6 · due Wednesday, 15 July 2026 · interactive page + PDF write-upOverviewThe sequel to Assignment 5: the two items struck from that problem set become this one. In all four models—normal with known variance, Poisson, exponential in the mean, and the exponential family parameterized by θ = ψ′(ξ)—the maximum likelihood estimator is the sample mean, its variance is exact rather than asymptotic, and it sits on the variance bound; the fourth problem shows why the instructor's choice of parameterization makes the likelihood equation hand over the estimator directly. Interactive: the likelihood equation picking out ȳ, the estimator's sampling distribution against the bound, and the slope-matching picture on ψ.
  • Homework 7Three Bayes EstimatorsAssignment 7 · due Thursday, 16 July 2026 · interactive page + PDF write-upOverviewThe Bayes estimator under squared-error loss in three conjugate models—normal data with a normal prior, Bernoulli with Beta, Poisson with Gamma—each found by matching kernels and taking the posterior mean. All three come out as the same weighted average: sample mean against prior mean, with the prior worth σ²/τ² observations, α+β pseudo-trials, or β units of exposure, and the data buying out the prior as n grows. Interactive: three conjugate-update laboratories with prior, likelihood, and posterior live.
