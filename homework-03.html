Risks and Bayes risks: the two-sample trial, and a laboratory for choosing priors

Assignment 3 works the class example—two Bernoulli arms, squared-error loss, independent uniform priors—and a new normal problem whose shrinkage family \((1-a)\,S/n + a\) contains the Bayes rule as one member. The write-up carries the derivations; these two laboratories let you steer the sample sizes, the shrinkage weight, and the prior itself, and watch every formula respond.

Problem 1 · The two-sample Bernoulli trial

Rates against shaded rates

Two estimators of \( \theta_T-\theta_P \): the difference of observed rates \(\hat\delta\) (unbiased) and the difference of shaded rates \(\tilde\delta\), built from \((1+X)/(2+n)\) in each arm. The risks live on the square \((\theta_T,\theta_P)\in[0,1]^2\); the two panels slice it along equal rates and opposite rates. Under independent uniform priors the Bayes risks are \( \tfrac16\!\left(\tfrac1{n_T}+\tfrac1{n_P}\right) \) against \( \tfrac16\!\left(\tfrac1{n_T+2}+\tfrac1{n_P+2}\right) \)—the shaded rule wins for every pair of arm sizes, because it is the Bayes rule.

equal rates: θ_T = θ_P = θ
opposite rates: θ_P = 1 − θ_T
\(R_{\hat\delta}\) (observed rates) \(R_{\tilde\delta}\) (shaded rates)

With equal arms the shading biases cancel along the diagonal and the dashed curve sits strictly below—a pure variance gain; try unequal arms and watch the cancellation fail. On the anti-diagonal the biases reinforce and the rate difference wins near the corners: the surfaces cross, neither rule dominates, and only the prior's verdict—the Bayes risks above—breaks the tie.

Problem 2 · The normal location problem

Choosing the prior: a shrinkage laboratory

Data \(Y_1,\dots,Y_n\) i.i.d.\ \(N(\varphi,\sigma^2)\), \(\sigma^2\) known; prior \(\varphi\sim N(\mu_0, v_0^2)\); estimators \(d_a=(1-a)\,\bar Y + a\,\mu_0\). The risk of \(d_a\) is \((1-a)^2\sigma^2/n + a^2(\mu_0-\varphi)^2\), its Bayes risk is \(r(a)=(1-a)^2\sigma^2/n + a^2v_0^2\), and the minimizing weight is the prior's share of the total precision, $$ a^{*} \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2/n}{\,v_0^2+\sigma^2/n\,}, $$ at which \(d_{a^{*}}\) is the posterior mean and \(r(a^{*})\) the posterior variance. The assignment's case is \(\mu_0=1\), \(v_0=1\). Steer everything.

prior · likelihood of x̄ · posterior
data: \(R_{d_0}\), likelihood prior-side: \(R_{d_a}\), prior posterior

Three experiments worth running. Asymptotics: grow \(n\) and watch \(a^{*}\) fall toward zero—the data buy out the prior. Confidence: shrink \(v_0\) and watch \(a^{*}\) climb and the posterior hug the prior mean—a confident prior takes more of the average. Misplacement: drag \(\mu_0\) away from \(\bar x\): the machinery still averages, and the posterior sits between the two, which is exactly why the choice of prior is a modeling decision and not a formality.
