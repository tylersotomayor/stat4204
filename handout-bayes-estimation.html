B.1The Problem: Guessing a Number You Cannot See

Concept B.1.1
The estimation problem

A new treatment cures some fraction of the patients who receive it. Call that fraction \(\theta\): if \(\theta=0.7\), then in the long run \(70\%\) of treated patients are cured. Nobody knows \(\theta\). It is not random—it is a fixed fact about the treatment—but it is hidden: the only way to learn about it is to treat patients and watch what happens. Suppose we treat \(n=10\) patients and \(x=7\) of them are cured. What number should we now report as our best guess of \(\theta\)? That question—turn data into a single defensible guess at a hidden number—is called estimation, and this handout builds one complete, principled answer to it.

Concept B.1.2
Two sources of information

The obvious answer is the observed cure rate, \(7/10=0.7\). It is a good answer, and by the end of the handout you will know exactly in what sense. But notice that the data are not the only thing you know. Perhaps treatments of this kind have historically cured between \(30\%\) and \(60\%\) of patients; perhaps the biology says a cure rate near \(1\) is implausible. Ten patients is not many, and it seems wasteful to ignore everything except those ten outcomes. The Bayesian approach—the subject of this handout—is defined by one move: it expresses both sources of information, the data and the background knowledge, in the same language, the language of probability. Once both speak that language, a single theorem combines them optimally.

Remark B.1.3
The route

The handout runs in a straight line, and it helps to see the whole road first. §B.2 builds the probability vocabulary (random variables, distributions, expectation, conditional distributions)—the language everything else is written in. §B.3 makes “estimation” precise and shows why, without background knowledge, the problem gets stuck: candidate guessing rules cannot be ranked. §B.4 introduces the Bayesian move—treat the hidden number as random, with a distribution expressing what you believe about it—and derives Bayes' theorem, the rule for updating that belief with data. §B.5 proves the punchline: once beliefs are updated, the best single-number guess under the natural error measure is the average of the updated belief. §§B.6–B.9 then work three complete examples with real numbers—a fraction, a measurement, and a count—and close with the one formula all three share: the Bayes estimate as a linear combination of the prior's guess and the data's average. §B.10 asks the honest question—where did the prior belief come from?—before §B.11 hands you the standard vocabulary for further reading.

B.2The Probability Toolkit, from Zero

Probability is the mathematics of quantities whose values you cannot predict. This section defines the six ideas the rest of the handout uses—random variable, distribution, expectation, variance, conditional distribution, conditional expectation—each with a small computation so the definition is not just words. Readers who have met these before can skim, but should slow down at B.2.7 and B.2.9, which carry the whole later argument.

Definition B.2.1
Random variable; realization

A random variable is a numerical quantity whose value is determined by the outcome of something uncertain—a measurement not yet made. The number of cures among ten patients, before the trial runs, is a random variable; so is tomorrow's temperature. Random variables are written with capital letters (\(X\), \(Y\)); once the uncertainty resolves and a value is observed, that observed number—written in lowercase (\(x\), \(y\))—is called the realization. “\(X=7\)” is an event that had some probability in advance; “\(x=7\)” is what actually happened.

Definition B.2.2
Distribution; pmf; density

The distribution of a random variable is the complete advance description of how likely it is to fall anywhere: the assignment of a probability to every statement of the form “\(X\) lands in the set \(A\).” For a discrete random variable (one taking isolated values, like a count) the distribution is carried by the probability mass function (pmf) $$ p(x) \;=\; P(X = x), $$ the probability of each individual value; probabilities of sets are found by adding: \( P(X\in A)=\sum_{x\in A}p(x) \). For a continuous random variable (one taking values in a continuum, like a fraction in \([0,1]\)) individual points all have probability zero, and the distribution is instead carried by a density \(f(x)\ge0\), with probabilities found by integrating:1 $$ P(X\in A) \;=\; \int_A f(x)\,dx. $$ In both cases the total is \(1\): \(\sum_x p(x)=1\), \(\int f(x)\,dx=1\)—something must happen.

Definition B.2.3
Expectation

The expectation (or mean) of a random variable \(X\) is its probability-weighted average value, $$ \E\{X\} \;=\; \sum_x x\, p(x) \quad\text{(discrete)}, \qquad \E\{X\} \;=\; \int x\, f(x)\, dx \quad\text{(continuous)}: $$ each possible value, weighted by how likely it is. Geometrically it is the balancing point of the distribution: if the pmf or density were a physical distribution of weight along a rod, \(\E\{X\}\) is where the rod balances. It is the single number that best summarizes “where the distribution sits”—a claim §B.5 will turn from slogan into theorem.

Example B.2.4
Two expectations, fully computed

(a) A random variable \(Z\) takes the value \(1\) with probability \(\theta\) and \(0\) with probability \(1-\theta\) (one patient: cured or not; such a \(Z\) is called a Bernoulli(\(\theta\)) variable). Its expectation, straight from the definition: $$ \E\{Z\} \;=\; 1\cdot P(Z=1) + 0\cdot P(Z=0) \;=\; 1\cdot\theta + 0\cdot(1-\theta) \;=\; \theta. $$ The mean of a \(0\)-or-\(1\) variable is its probability of being \(1\)—the bridge between “probability” and “long-run fraction.”

(b) A random variable \(W\) takes values \(1, 2, 6\) with probabilities \(\tfrac12, \tfrac13, \tfrac16\). Then $$ \E\{W\} \;=\; 1\cdot\tfrac12 + 2\cdot\tfrac13 + 6\cdot\tfrac16 \;=\; \tfrac12 + \tfrac23 + 1 \;=\; \tfrac{3+4+6}{6} \;=\; \tfrac{13}{6} \;\approx\; 2.17. $$ Note the balancing-point reading: \(2.17\) is not a possible value of \(W\); it is where the weights balance. Expectation also respects sums and constant multiples—\( \E\{aX+bY\}=a\,\E\{X\}+b\,\E\{Y\} \), always, with no assumptions about how \(X\) and \(Y\) are related—a property called linearity that we will use constantly.2

Definition B.2.5
Variance; standard deviation

The variance of \(X\) measures its spread: the expected squared distance from its own mean, $$ \Var(X) \;=\; \E\big\{ (X - \E\{X\})^2 \big\}. $$ Squaring makes deviations positive (so left- and right-misses cannot cancel) and charges large deviations disproportionately. Its square root, the standard deviation, is the spread in the original units. A useful equivalent form, obtained by expanding the square and using linearity: $$ \Var(X) \;=\; \E\big\{X^2 - 2X\,\E\{X\} + \E\{X\}^2\big\} \;=\; \E\{X^2\} - 2\,\E\{X\}\,\E\{X\} + \E\{X\}^2, $$ that is, $$ \Var(X) \;=\; \E\{X^2\} - \E\{X\}^2. $$ For the Bernoulli variable of B.2.4(a): \(Z^2=Z\) (since \(0^2=0\), \(1^2=1\)), so \(\E\{Z^2\}=\theta\) and $$ \Var(Z) \;=\; \theta - \theta^2 \;=\; \theta(1-\theta), $$ largest at \(\theta=\tfrac12\) (a fair coin is the most unpredictable coin) and zero at \(\theta=0\) or \(1\) (no uncertainty at all).

Definition B.2.6
Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions

For a pair of random variables \((X,Y)\), the joint distribution assigns a probability to every combination: in the discrete case, \(p(x,y)=P(X=x,\,Y=y)\). From it, the distribution of each variable alone—the marginal—is recovered by summing out the other: $$ p_X(x) \;=\; \sum_y p(x,y). $$ And the conditional distribution of \(X\) given that \(Y\) has been observed to equal \(y\) is the joint, restricted to that observation and rescaled to total 1: $$ p(x \mid y) \;=\; \frac{p(x,y)}{p_Y(y)} \qquad (p_Y(y)>0). $$ Read the formula as a two-step act: take the slice of the joint where \(Y=y\) (the numerator), then renormalize it into a genuine probability distribution by dividing by the slice's total mass (the denominator). For continuous variables the same formulas hold with densities and integrals.

Example B.2.7
A worked table—and, secretly, the whole handout

A disease affects \(10\%\) of a population. A screening test comes back positive for \(90\%\) of the sick and (falsely) for \(20\%\) of the healthy. Let \(D=1\) mean sick, \(T=1\) mean the test is positive. The joint distribution, built by multiplying each group's size by its test behavior: $$ \begin{array}{c|cc|c} & T=1 & T=0 & \text{row total}\\ \hline D=1 & 0.10\times0.90 = 0.09 & 0.10\times0.10 = 0.01 & 0.10\\ D=0 & 0.90\times0.20 = 0.18 & 0.90\times0.80 = 0.72 & 0.90\\ \hline \text{column total} & 0.27 & 0.73 & 1.00 \end{array} $$ The four cells total \(1\), the row totals are the marginal of \(D\), the column totals the marginal of \(T\). Now suppose your test comes back positive. What is the probability you are sick? Apply B.2.6: slice the \(T=1\) column, then renormalize by its total \(0.27\): $$ P(D=1 \mid T=1) \;=\; \frac{p(1,1)}{p_T(1)} \;=\; \frac{0.09}{0.27} \;=\; \frac13. $$ Pause on what just happened. Before the test, your probability of being sick was \(0.10\). You received data (a positive test), and your probability updated to \(0.33\)—higher, but nowhere near certainty, because false positives are common and the disease is rare. This little table is the entire Bayesian method in miniature: a belief, a data-generating mechanism, an observation, an updated belief. §B.4 will redo this computation with a continuous unknown in place of \(D\) and give its parts their proper names.

Definition B.2.8
Conditional expectation

The conditional expectation of \(X\) given \(Y=y\) is the mean of the conditional distribution: $$ \E\{X \mid Y=y\} \;=\; \sum_x x\, p(x\mid y) $$ (integral against the conditional density in the continuous case). It is the balancing point of the slice: your updated best summary of \(X\) after seeing \(Y=y\). Note that it is a number for each \(y\)—so, before \(Y\) is observed, the recipe \(y\mapsto\E\{X\mid Y=y\}\) is itself a random quantity, written \(\E\{X\mid Y\}\).

Theorem B.2.9
Iterated expectations

For jointly distributed \((X,Y)\) with \(\E\{|X|\}<\infty\), $$ \E\{X\} \;=\; \E\big\{\, \E\{X \mid Y\} \,\big\}: $$ the overall mean is the probability-weighted average, over what \(Y\) might be, of the slice-by-slice means.

Proof B.2.9
Proof of Iterated Expectations by Regrouping a Double Sum.
  1. Write out the right side from the definitions. The outer expectation averages the function \(y\mapsto\E\{X\mid Y=y\}\) against the marginal of \(Y\): $$ \E\big\{ \E\{X\mid Y\} \big\} \;=\; \sum_y \E\{X \mid Y=y\}\; p_Y(y) \;=\; \sum_y \Big( \sum_x x\, p(x\mid y) \Big) p_Y(y). $$
  2. Substitute the definition of the conditional pmf, \(p(x\mid y)=p(x,y)/p_Y(y)\): $$ \sum_y \sum_x x\,\frac{p(x,y)}{p_Y(y)}\; p_Y(y) \;=\; \sum_y \sum_x x\, p(x,y), $$ the marginal \(p_Y(y)\) cancelling exactly—this cancellation is the whole proof.
  3. Regroup the double sum by \(x\) (legal because the sum converges absolutely): $$ \sum_x x \sum_y p(x,y) \;=\; \sum_x x\, p_X(x) \;=\; \E\{X\}, $$ the inner sum being the marginal of \(X\) by B.2.6.
Uses B.2.6, B.2.8.

Why this theorem matters here: it lets you compute a complicated overall average by conditioning—break the world into slices where things are simple, average within each slice, then average the slice-answers. The main theorem of §B.5 is exactly one application of this maneuver.

B.3Estimation as a Decision, and Why It Gets Stuck

The toolkit is assembled; now aim it at the trial of B.1.1. This section makes four ideas precise—model, estimator, loss, risk—and then demonstrates the problem that motivates everything after: without background knowledge, competing estimators cannot be ranked.

Definition B.3.1
The statistical model, and the trial's

A statistical model is a family of candidate distributions for the data, indexed by the unknown quantity: for each possible value of \(\theta\), a statement of how the data would then behave. The unknown \(\theta\) is called the parameter; the set \(\Theta\) of its possible values, the parameter space. For the trial: each of \(n\) patients is independently cured with probability \(\theta\), and \(X\) counts the cures. A particular sequence with \(x\) specified cures and \(n-x\) specified failures has probability \(\theta^x(1-\theta)^{n-x}\) (independence multiplies the per-patient probabilities), and there are \(\binom{n}{x}\) ways to choose which \(x\) patients are the cured ones, so $$ f(x \mid \theta) \;=\; P(X = x \mid \theta) \;=\; \binom{n}{x}\,\theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{\,n-x}, \qquad x = 0, 1, \dots, n, $$ with \(\Theta=[0,1]\). This family is the binomial model, written \(X\sim\text{Binomial}(n,\theta)\).3

Definition B.3.2
Estimator

An estimator is a rule—a function \(a(\cdot)\), committed to before the data arrive—that turns any possible data set into a guess: observe \(X=x\), report \(a(x)\). The estimator is the recipe; the estimate is the number the recipe produces on your data. Candidate recipes for the trial are cheap to write down: report the observed rate \(a_1(x)=x/n\); ignore the data and always report \(a_2(x)=\tfrac12\); split the difference, \(a_3(x)=(x+1)/(n+2)\). Each produces an answer on every data set, so producing answers cannot be what makes an estimator good. We need a way to score them.

Definition B.3.3
Loss function; squared-error loss

A loss function \(L(\theta,a)\) prices the consequence of reporting the guess \(a\) when the truth is \(\theta\): bigger loss, worse outcome. It is chosen by us, when we pose the problem, as the definition of what counts as a miss. This handout uses the standard choice for estimating a number, squared-error loss: $$ L(\theta, a) \;=\; (\theta - a)^2. $$ Why this and not, say, \(|\theta-a|\)? Three reasons, in increasing order of importance: squaring is symmetric (over- and under-estimation are punished alike); it charges large misses disproportionately (missing by \(0.2\) costs four times missing by \(0.1\)), which matches how bad decisions usually scale; and—the deep reason, proved in §B.5—it is the loss under which averages are the optimal guesses, making the whole theory computable. Different losses lead to different optimal summaries; squared error leads to means.4

Definition B.3.4
Risk

Fix a candidate truth \(\theta\) and an estimator \(a(\cdot)\). Because the data are random, the loss you will suffer is random too; its expectation, computed under the model at that \(\theta\), is the risk: $$ R(\theta) \;=\; \E\big\{ L(\theta, a(X)) \big\} \;=\; \E\big\{ (\theta - a(X))^2 \big\} \qquad\text{(\(X\) distributed per \(f(\cdot\mid\theta)\))}. $$ The risk answers: if the truth were \(\theta\), how badly would this recipe do on average? Note carefully that it is a function of \(\theta\)—one number per candidate truth—because the truth is exactly what we do not know.

Example B.3.5
The risk of the observed rate, computed in full

Take \(a_1(X)=X/n\), the observed rate, under the binomial model. First its mean. Writing \(X=Z_1+\cdots+Z_n\) as the sum of the \(n\) per-patient Bernoulli indicators and using linearity (B.2.4): $$ \E\{X\} \;=\; \E\{Z_1\} + \cdots + \E\{Z_n\} \;=\; \underbrace{\theta+\cdots+\theta}_{n} \;=\; n\theta, \qquad\text{so}\qquad \E\Big\{\frac{X}{n}\Big\} \;=\; \frac{n\theta}{n} \;=\; \theta. $$ The rate is centered on the truth at every \(\theta\) (such an estimator is called unbiased). Its risk is therefore its variance: $$ R_1(\theta) \;=\; \E\Big\{ \Big(\frac{X}{n}-\theta\Big)^{\!2} \Big\} \;=\; \E\Big\{ \Big(\frac{X}{n}-\E\Big\{\frac{X}{n}\Big\}\Big)^{\!2} \Big\} \;=\; \Var\Big(\frac{X}{n}\Big), $$ by the definition of variance (B.2.5) with the mean \(\theta\) substituted. Variances of independent summands add,5 and each Bernoulli indicator has variance \(\theta(1-\theta)\) (B.2.5), so $$ \Var(X) \;=\; n\,\theta(1-\theta) \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad R_1(\theta) \;=\; \Var\Big(\frac{X}{n}\Big) \;=\; \frac{\Var(X)}{n^2} \;=\; \frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{n}. $$

Example B.3.6
The stuck comparison

Now score the data-ignoring rule \(a_2\equiv\tfrac12\). Its loss is not random at all—it reports \(\tfrac12\) no matter what—so its risk is just $$ R_2(\theta) \;=\; \big(\theta - \tfrac12\big)^2. $$ Compare the two at \(n=10\). At \(\theta=\tfrac12\): $$ R_1\big(\tfrac12\big) \;=\; \frac{\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac12}{10} \;=\; 0.025, \qquad R_2\big(\tfrac12\big) \;=\; 0: $$ the stubborn rule is unbeatable—it happens to be guessing the truth exactly. But at \(\theta=0.9\): $$ R_1(0.9) \;=\; \frac{0.9\times0.1}{10} \;=\; 0.009, \qquad R_2(0.9) \;=\; (0.9-0.5)^2 \;=\; 0.16: $$ now the stubborn rule is nearly eighteen times worse. Neither estimator wins at every \(\theta\); their risk curves cross. And this is the general situation, not a quirk of a silly example: reasonable estimators trade performance in one region of \(\Theta\) for performance in another, and “minimize the risk” is not an instruction anyone can follow, because there is no single risk number to minimize—there is a curve, and curves are not ordered. The problem is stuck. To get unstuck we must be willing to say which values of \(\theta\) we care about more—to put weights on the candidate truths. Doing that with a probability distribution is the Bayesian move, and it is the next section.

B.4The Prior, the Posterior, and Bayes' Theorem

Definition B.4.1
Prior distribution

A prior distribution is a probability distribution \(\pi(\theta)\) placed on the parameter itself, expressing what is believed about \(\theta\) before the data are seen: regions of \(\Theta\) where \(\pi\) is large are values considered plausible, regions where \(\pi\) is small, implausible. Formally this is a change of status: \(\theta\) is now treated as a random variable with density (or pmf) \(\pi\), so that all of §B.2's machinery—joints, slices, conditional expectations—applies to it. The prior is an input, chosen by the analyst as a model of background knowledge, exactly as the binomial model was chosen as a model of the data mechanism. It is not derived from the data and it is not the thing we will optimize; it is a premise.6

Definition B.4.2
The joint distribution of parameter and data

Once \(\theta\) is random, the pair \((\theta, X)\) has a joint distribution, and it is built from the two models already on the table, prior first, data mechanism second: $$ \text{joint}(\theta, x) \;=\; \pi(\theta)\; f(x \mid \theta). $$ Read it generatively: nature draws a truth \(\theta\) from \(\pi\), then the experiment draws data \(x\) from the model at that truth. (This is the same construction as the table of B.2.7: row weights \(0.1/0.9\) were the prior over \(D\); the within-row test behavior was the model; cells were prior \(\times\) model.)

Definition B.4.3
The marginal distribution of the data

Summing (or integrating) the joint over \(\theta\) gives the marginal distribution of the data, $$ m(x) \;=\; \int_{\Theta} \pi(\theta)\, f(x\mid\theta)\, d\theta: $$ how probable the data set \(x\) is overall, averaged over what the truth might be. In the table it was the column totals. It involves no unknown—\(\theta\) has been integrated away—and its role in what follows is purely that of a normalizing constant.

Theorem B.4.4
Bayes' theorem

The conditional distribution of the parameter given the observed data—the posterior distribution—is $$ \pi(\theta \mid x) \;=\; \frac{\pi(\theta)\, f(x\mid\theta)}{m(x)} \;=\; \frac{\pi(\theta)\, f(x\mid\theta)} {\displaystyle\int_{\Theta} \pi(t)\, f(x\mid t)\, dt}. $$

Proof B.4.4
Proof of Bayes' Theorem by Slicing the Joint.
  1. By the definition of a conditional distribution (B.2.6), applied to the pair \((\theta, X)\) with the roles “condition on the second coordinate”: the conditional of \(\theta\) given \(X=x\) is the joint divided by the marginal of the conditioning variable, $$ \pi(\theta\mid x) \;=\; \frac{\text{joint}(\theta,x)}{m(x)}. $$
  2. Substitute the joint's factorization \(\pi(\theta)f(x\mid\theta)\) (B.4.2) in the numerator and the integral form of \(m(x)\) (B.4.3) in the denominator. (The dummy variable in the denominator is renamed \(t\) so it cannot be confused with the free \(\theta\) in the numerator.)
Uses B.2.6, B.4.2, B.4.3.
Remark B.4.5
Reading Bayes' theorem

Three observations, each load-bearing. First, the structure is slice and renormalize, nothing more: observing \(x\) freezes one slice of the joint; dividing by the slice's total mass \(m(x)\) makes it a probability distribution over \(\theta\) again. The theorem is B.2.6 wearing new labels. Second, look at how the data enter: only through the factor \(f(x\mid\theta)\), read as a function of \(\theta\) with the observed \(x\) plugged in. That function has a name—the likelihood—and it scores each candidate truth by how well it explains what was actually seen. Posterior \(\propto\) prior \(\times\) likelihood: updated belief is old belief reweighted by explanatory success. Third, the denominator does not involve \(\theta\) at all. So to identify the posterior it is enough to know the numerator's shape in \(\theta\); the constant is whatever makes the total \(1\). In practice one therefore writes $$ \pi(\theta\mid x) \;\propto\; \pi(\theta)\, f(x\mid\theta), $$ drops every factor not involving \(\theta\), and tries to recognize the resulting shape as a known family. §B.7 runs exactly this play, and the integral in the denominator never gets computed.

Example B.4.6
The table of B.2.7, renamed

You have already done a full Bayesian update. In B.2.7: the prior was \(\pi(1)=P(D=1)=0.10\); the model was the test's behavior given the disease state, \(f(1\mid D{=}1)=0.9\) and \(f(1\mid D{=}0)=0.2\); the observed data was \(T=1\); the marginal was \(m(1)=0.10\times0.9+0.90\times0.2=0.27\); and Bayes' theorem gave the posterior $$ \pi(1\mid 1) \;=\; \frac{\pi(1)\,f(1\mid1)}{m(1)} \;=\; \frac{0.10\times0.90}{0.27} \;=\; \frac{0.09}{0.27} \;=\; \frac13. $$ Belief \(0.10\), data, belief \(\tfrac13\). Everything from here on is this computation with a continuous unknown—and then one further question the table never asked: once you have the posterior, what single number should you report?

B.5The Best Guess Under Squared Error

The posterior is a whole distribution over \(\theta\)—a full account of what is now believed. Often that is the honest thing to report. But estimation demands one number, and now the loss function chooses it. This section proves the two results on which everything rests: with no data, the best single-number guess of a random quantity is its mean; with data, the best guess is the mean of the posterior. The first result is the engine; the second is one application of iterated expectations to it.

Theorem B.5.1
The best constant guess is the mean

Let \(\theta\) be a random variable with \(\E\{\theta^2\}<\infty\). Among all constants \(a\), the expected squared error $$ \E\big\{ (\theta - a)^2 \big\} \;=\; \Var(\theta) + \big( \E\{\theta\} - a \big)^2 $$ is minimized at exactly one point: \(a = \E\{\theta\}\), where it equals \(\Var(\theta)\).

Proof B.5.1
Proof of the Best Constant by Adding and Subtracting the Mean.
  1. Write \(\mu=\E\{\theta\}\) for brevity, and route the error through \(\mu\) by adding and subtracting it—the step that makes the square split cleanly: $$ \theta - a \;=\; (\theta - \mu) + (\mu - a). $$
  2. Square the two-term sum using \((u+v)^2=u^2+2uv+v^2\): $$ (\theta-a)^2 \;=\; (\theta-\mu)^2 \;+\; 2(\theta-\mu)(\mu-a) \;+\; (\mu-a)^2. $$
  3. Take expectations term by term (linearity, B.2.4). The first term gives \(\E\{(\theta-\mu)^2\}=\Var(\theta)\) by definition. In the middle term, \(\mu-a\) is a constant and factors out: $$ \E\big\{2(\theta-\mu)(\mu-a)\big\} \;=\; 2(\mu-a)\,\E\{\theta-\mu\} \;=\; 2(\mu-a)\big(\E\{\theta\}-\mu\big) \;=\; 2(\mu-a)\cdot 0 \;=\; 0: $$ deviations from one's own mean average to zero, so the cross term dies. The last term is the constant \((\mu-a)^2\), its own expectation.
  4. What remains is the display: \(\E\{(\theta-a)^2\} = \Var(\theta) + (\mu-a)^2\). The first piece does not involve \(a\) at all; the second is a square, so it is \(\ge 0\) always, \(=0\) exactly at \(a=\mu\), and \(>0\) elsewhere. The minimizer is \(a=\mu\), uniquely.
Uses B.2.3, B.2.4, B.2.5.
Remark B.5.2
What the identity says

The proof delivered more than a minimizer; it delivered an exact accounting. Guessing a random quantity with a constant costs you \(\Var(\theta)\) no matter what—the irreducible price of the quantity's own randomness—plus a penalty \((\E\{\theta\}-a)^2\) for centering your guess anywhere but the mean. This is why “balancing point” was the right picture for the expectation in B.2.3: under squared error, the balancing point is provably the best single summary. And note the theorem's scope: any random \(\theta\), any distribution with a finite second moment. In particular it will apply verbatim to \(\theta\) under its posterior distribution—which is the next theorem.

Definition B.5.3
Bayes risk

Let \(a(\cdot)\) be an estimator. Its Bayes risk is its expected squared error when both sources of randomness run: the truth \(\theta\) drawn from the prior, the data \(X\) drawn from the model given that truth—that is, \((\theta,X)\) carrying the joint distribution of B.4.2: $$ r(a) \;=\; \E\big\{ \big( a(X) - \theta \big)^2 \big\}. $$ Compare B.3.4: the risk froze \(\theta\) and averaged over data only, producing a curve; the Bayes risk averages over \(\theta\) as well—the prior supplies precisely the weights over candidate truths that B.3.6 found missing—and produces a single number per estimator. Numbers, unlike curves, can be minimized. The estimator with the smallest Bayes risk is called the Bayes estimator, and the next theorem identifies it.

Theorem B.5.4
The Bayes estimator is the posterior mean

Assume \(\E\{\theta^2\}<\infty\). Among all estimators \(a(\cdot)\), the Bayes risk \(r(a)\) is minimized by reporting, for each possible data set \(x\), the mean of the posterior distribution: $$ a^{*}(x) \;=\; \E\{\theta \mid X = x\} \;=\; \int_{\Theta} \theta\; \pi(\theta\mid x)\, d\theta. $$

Proof B.5.4
Proof of the Posterior-Mean Rule by Conditioning on the Data.
  1. Iterate the Bayes risk through the data, using iterated expectations (B.2.9) with the conditioning variable \(X\): $$ r(a) \;=\; \E\big\{ (a(X)-\theta)^2 \big\} \;=\; \E\Big[\, \E\big\{ (a(X)-\theta)^2 \bigm| X \big\} \,\Big]: $$ the overall average equals the average, over data sets, of the within-data-set averages.
  2. Freeze one slice \(X=x\) and look at the inner expectation. Inside the slice, \(a(x)\) is a fixed constant—the recipe's output on that data set—while \(\theta\) remains random, distributed according to the conditional distribution of \(\theta\) given \(X=x\). And that conditional distribution is, by B.4.4, exactly the posterior \(\pi(\cdot\mid x)\). So the inner expectation is the expected squared error of a constant guess at a random quantity: $$ \E\big\{ (a(x) - \theta)^2 \bigm| X=x \big\} \;=\; \E\big\{ (\theta - c)^2 \big\}\Big|_{c=a(x)} \quad\text{with \(\theta\) carrying the posterior.} $$
  3. Apply B.5.1 to the posterior distribution—the theorem holds for any distribution, so in particular this one. Within the slice, the inner expectation is minimized, uniquely, by the constant equal to that distribution's mean: $$ c \;=\; \E\{\theta \mid X = x\}, $$ and no other choice of \(a(x)\) does as well in this slice.
  4. The rule \(a^{*}(x)=\E\{\theta\mid X=x\}\) therefore minimizes the inner expectation simultaneously at every \(x\). Averaging over slices (step 1) preserves the domination: for any competitor \(a\), each of \(a\)'s slice-averages is at least \(a^{*}\)'s, so the overall average is too. Hence \(r(a^{*})\le r(a)\) for every estimator \(a\), which is the claim.
Uses B.2.9, B.4.4, B.5.1.
Remark B.5.5
The recipe, in words

The theorem compresses the whole method into two verbs: update, then average.

1. Model the data mechanism (\(f(x\mid\theta)\)) and state your prior (\(\pi\)).
2. Observe \(x\); form the posterior \(\pi(\theta\mid x)\propto\pi(\theta)f(x\mid\theta)\) (B.4.4), the updated belief.
3. Report the posterior's mean. That single number minimizes expected squared error among all possible estimators, given the premises.

Notice what is chosen and what is given: the prior and the loss are premises, posed with the problem; the theorem then leaves nothing to choose—the optimal estimator is determined. The remaining work in any particular problem is computational: actually finding the posterior and its mean. The next three sections do that work twice, in the two settings where it is fully explicit.

B.6A Toolkit Distribution: the Beta Family

To run the recipe on the trial we need a family of prior distributions for a fraction—a random quantity living in \([0,1]\)—rich enough to express real beliefs and tame enough to compute with. The standard choice is the Beta family. Its normalizing constant involves the Gamma function, so that comes first; both objects repay the ten minutes they take to set up.

Definition B.6.1
The Gamma function

For \(s>0\), $$ \Gamma(s) \;=\; \int_0^{\infty} t^{\,s-1}\, e^{-t}\, dt. $$ Two facts are all we need. First, the recursion \(\Gamma(s+1)=s\,\Gamma(s)\): integrate by parts with \(u=t^{s}\) (so \(du=s\,t^{s-1}dt\)) and \(dv=e^{-t}dt\) (so \(v=-e^{-t}\)): $$ \Gamma(s+1) \;=\; \int_0^{\infty} t^{s} e^{-t}\, dt \;=\; \Big[ -t^{s} e^{-t} \Big]_0^{\infty} + s\int_0^{\infty} t^{\,s-1} e^{-t}\, dt \;=\; 0 + s\,\Gamma(s), $$ the boundary term vanishing at both ends (\(t^s e^{-t}\to0\) as \(t\to\infty\) because the exponential beats any power; at \(t=0\), \(t^s=0\) for \(s>0\)). Second, the anchor \(\Gamma(1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-t}dt=1\). Together they give, for whole numbers, \(\Gamma(n) = (n-1)\,\Gamma(n-1) = \cdots = (n-1)!\): the Gamma function is the factorial, shifted by one and extended to non-integers.

Definition B.6.2
The Beta distribution

For parameters \(\alpha>0\), \(\beta>0\), the Beta(\(\alpha,\beta\)) distribution is the continuous distribution on \([0,1]\) with density $$ \pi(\theta) \;=\; \frac{\theta^{\,\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\,\beta-1}}{\mathrm{B}(\alpha,\beta)}, \qquad \mathrm{B}(\alpha,\beta) \;=\; \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\,\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}, $$ the constant \(\mathrm{B}(\alpha,\beta)\) being exactly what makes the density integrate to \(1\). The exponents steer the shape: mass shifts toward \(1\) as \(\alpha\) grows and toward \(0\) as \(\beta\) grows, so \((\alpha,\beta)\) behave like counts of imagined prior successes and failures—a reading B.7.3 will make exact. One member deserves special mention: \(\alpha=\beta=1\) gives \(\pi(\theta)=\theta^{0}(1-\theta)^{0}/\mathrm{B}(1,1)\), and since \(\mathrm{B}(1,1)=\Gamma(1)\Gamma(1)/\Gamma(2)=1\cdot1/1=1\), this is \(\pi(\theta)\equiv1\): the uniform distribution, the flat prior that treats every fraction as equally plausible.

Theorem B.6.3
The mean of a Beta distribution

If \(\theta\sim\) Beta(\(\alpha,\beta\)), then $$ \E\{\theta\} \;=\; \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}. $$

Proof B.6.3
Proof of the Beta Mean by the Kernel Trick.
  1. From the definition of expectation, multiplying the density by \(\theta\) merges the extra \(\theta\) into the exponent: $$ \E\{\theta\} \;=\; \int_0^1 \theta\cdot \frac{\theta^{\,\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\,\beta-1}}{\mathrm{B}(\alpha,\beta)}\,d\theta \;=\; \frac{1}{\mathrm{B}(\alpha,\beta)} \int_0^1 \theta^{\,(\alpha+1)-1}(1-\theta)^{\,\beta-1}\,d\theta. $$
  2. Recognize the remaining integrand as the unnormalized Beta(\(\alpha+1,\beta\)) density. Its integral must therefore be that family's normalizing constant: $$ \int_0^1 \theta^{\,(\alpha+1)-1}(1-\theta)^{\,\beta-1}\,d\theta \;=\; \mathrm{B}(\alpha+1,\beta). $$ (This maneuver—evaluate an integral by recognizing a known density's shape—is the kernel trick, and it is the workhorse of every Bayesian computation to come.)
  3. So \(\E\{\theta\}=\mathrm{B}(\alpha+1,\beta)/\mathrm{B}(\alpha,\beta)\). Expand both via B.6.2 and cancel: $$ \E\{\theta\} \;=\; \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\,\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)}\cdot \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\,\Gamma(\beta)} \;=\; \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\cdot \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)}. $$
  4. Apply the recursion \(\Gamma(s+1)=s\,\Gamma(s)\) (B.6.1) to each ratio: \(\Gamma(\alpha+1)/\Gamma(\alpha)=\alpha\) and \(\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)/\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)=1/(\alpha+\beta)\). Hence \(\E\{\theta\}=\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)\).
Uses B.2.3, B.6.1, B.6.2.

B.7The Cure Rate, Start to Finish

Everything is on the table. Model: \(X\sim\) Binomial(\(n,\theta\)) (B.3.1). Prior: to begin, the flat Beta(1,1)—“before the trial, no fraction is privileged over any other.” Loss: squared error. Data: \(x=7\) cures among \(n=10\) patients. The recipe of B.5.5 says: form the posterior, report its mean.

Example B.7.1
The posterior, by kernel-matching

Bayes' theorem in its proportional form (B.4.5), for a general \(x\) and \(n\) under the flat prior: $$ \pi(\theta \mid x) \;\propto\; \pi(\theta)\, f(x\mid\theta) \;=\; 1 \cdot \binom{n}{x}\,\theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{\,n-x} \;\propto\; \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{\,n-x}, $$ the binomial coefficient dropping because it does not involve \(\theta\)—it is data-only, hence absorbed into the normalizing constant. Now match the kernel: rewrite the exponents in Beta form, $$ \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{\,n-x} \;=\; \theta^{\,(x+1)-1}\,(1-\theta)^{\,(n-x+1)-1}, $$ which is precisely the shape of a Beta(\(x+1,\; n-x+1\)) density (B.6.2). A density's shape determines it (the constant is forced by total mass 1), so $$ \theta \mid X = x \;\sim\; \text{Beta}\big(x+1,\; n-x+1\big), $$ and the normalizing integral \(m(x)\) of B.4.3 was never touched. By B.6.3, the Bayes estimate—the posterior mean—is $$ a^{*}(x) \;=\; \E\{\theta\mid X=x\} \;=\; \frac{x+1}{(x+1)+(n-x+1)} \;=\; \frac{x+1}{n+2}. $$

Example B.7.2
The trial's numbers

With \(n=10\) and \(x=7\): the posterior is Beta(8,4), and the Bayes estimate is $$ a^{*}(7) \;=\; \frac{7+1}{10+2} \;=\; \frac{8}{12} \;=\; \frac{2}{3} \;\approx\; 0.667, $$ against the raw rate's \(x/n=0.700\). The posterior also prices its own uncertainty: the Beta(\(\alpha,\beta\)) variance is \(\alpha\beta/\big((\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)\big)\),7 here $$ \Var(\theta\mid X=7) \;=\; \frac{8\cdot4}{12^2\cdot13} \;=\; \frac{32}{1872} \;=\; \frac{2}{117} \;\approx\; 0.0171, \qquad \text{sd} \;\approx\; 0.131. $$ So the full Bayesian report is: the cure rate is around 0.67, give or take about 0.13—an estimate and an honest width, both read off one distribution.

Remark B.7.3
The anatomy of \((x+1)/(n+2)\): a weighted average

Rewrite the Bayes estimate to expose its structure: $$ \frac{x+1}{n+2} \;=\; \frac{2\cdot\tfrac12 \;+\; n\cdot\dfrac{x}{n}}{2+n}. $$ (Check: the numerator is \(1+x\) and the denominator \(2+n\), as before.) Read it as a weighted average of two guesses: the prior mean \(\tfrac12\), carrying weight 2, and the data's rate \(x/n\), carrying weight \(n\). The flat prior behaves exactly like two phantom patients, one cured and one not, mixed into the sample. The same algebra runs for any Beta(\(\alpha,\beta\)) prior: the posterior is Beta(\(\alpha+x,\,\beta+n-x\))—observed successes reinforce \(\alpha\), failures reinforce \(\beta\)—and its mean is $$ \frac{\alpha+x}{\alpha+\beta+n} \;=\; \frac{(\alpha+\beta)\cdot\dfrac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} \;+\; n\cdot\dfrac{x}{n}}{(\alpha+\beta)+n}: $$ prior mean weighted by \(\alpha+\beta\) (the prior is worth \(\alpha+\beta\) observations), data rate weighted by \(n\). This is the general shape of Bayesian estimates: a compromise between what you believed and what you saw, at exchange rates set by how much each side knows.

Remark B.7.5
Two sanity checks

No data. Set \(n=x=0\): the estimate is \((0+1)/(0+2)=\tfrac12\), the prior mean—with nothing observed, the recipe reports what you believed. All cures. (The raw rate \(x/n=1\) would assert exactly that.) This estimator for the all-successes case is old and famous—it is Laplace's rule of succession, his answer to “what probability that the sun rises tomorrow, given that it always has?”—and it falls out of the machinery here as a routine special case.

Under squared error the expected loss of a constant guess \(a\) is exactly B.5.1's identity $$ \E\{(\theta-a)^2 \mid x\} \;=\; \Var(\theta\mid x) \;+\; \big(\E\{\theta\mid x\}-a\big)^2, $$ a parabola with its floor at the posterior mean. B.8A Second Instrument: the Normal–Normal Pair

The Beta–binomial pair handled a fraction. The other workhorse setting is a measurement: a quantity on the whole line, observed through noise. One new distribution is needed.

Definition B.8.1
The normal distribution

The normal (or Gaussian) distribution \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) is the continuous distribution with density $$ f(y) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\, \exp\!\left( -\frac{(y-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right), \qquad y\in\mathbb{R}: $$ the bell curve, centered at its mean \(\mu\), with spread set by its standard deviation \(\sigma\) (about \(68\%\) of the probability lies within one \(\sigma\) of \(\mu\), about \(95\%\) within two). Its ubiquity has a theorem behind it (sums of many small independent effects are approximately normal—the central limit theorem, not needed here) and a practical reason: as a function of the variable in the exponent, the density is \(e^{-\text{quadratic}}\), and quadratics are closed under addition. That closure is exactly what the next example exploits.

Example B.8.2
Normal prior, normal measurement: the posterior in full

Setup. A quantity \(\theta\) (an aptitude score, a physical constant, a true weight) has prior \(\theta\sim N(m,\tau^2)\): best prior guess \(m\), prior uncertainty \(\tau\). An instrument measures it once with known noise level \(\sigma\): given the truth, \(X\mid\theta\sim N(\theta,\sigma^2)\). Observe \(X=x\). What is the posterior?

Multiply prior by likelihood (B.4.5), keeping only factors involving \(\theta\)—the two normalizing constants \(1/(\tau\sqrt{2\pi})\) and \(1/(\sigma\sqrt{2\pi})\) drop: $$ \pi(\theta\mid x) \;\propto\; \exp\!\left( -\frac{(\theta-m)^2}{2\tau^2} \right)\cdot \exp\!\left( -\frac{(x-\theta)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \;=\; \exp\!\left( -\frac12\left[ \frac{(\theta-m)^2}{\tau^2} + \frac{(\theta-x)^2}{\sigma^2} \right] \right). $$

Expand the bracket and collect powers of \(\theta\). Expanding each square, $$ \frac{\theta^2 - 2m\theta + m^2}{\tau^2} + \frac{\theta^2 - 2x\theta + x^2}{\sigma^2} \;=\; \theta^2\Big( \frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{1}{\sigma^2} \Big) \;-\; 2\theta\Big( \frac{m}{\tau^2}+\frac{x}{\sigma^2} \Big) \;+\; \Big( \frac{m^2}{\tau^2}+\frac{x^2}{\sigma^2} \Big), $$ and the last group is free of \(\theta\)—bound for the normalizing constant.

Complete the square. Abbreviate $$ \frac1v \;=\; \frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{1}{\sigma^2}, \qquad \frac{m^{*}}{v} \;=\; \frac{m}{\tau^2}+\frac{x}{\sigma^2} \quad\text{(defining \(v\) and \(m^*\))}, $$ so the \(\theta\)-dependent part is $$ \frac{\theta^2 - 2 m^{*}\theta}{v} \;=\; \frac{(\theta - m^{*})^2 - (m^{*})^2}{v}, $$ by adding and subtracting \((m^*)^2\) inside—and \((m^*)^2/v\), constant in \(\theta\), also departs for the normalizer. What remains is $$ \pi(\theta\mid x) \;\propto\; \exp\!\left( -\frac{(\theta-m^{*})^2}{2v} \right): $$ the kernel of a normal density (B.8.1). Matching shapes, $$ \theta \mid X=x \;\sim\; N\big( m^{*},\, v \big), \qquad m^{*} \;=\; v\Big( \frac{m}{\tau^2}+\frac{x}{\sigma^2} \Big) \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2\, m + \tau^2\, x}{\sigma^2+\tau^2}, $$ the last simplification by multiplying numerator and denominator by \(\sigma^2\tau^2\): $$ v\Big(\frac{m}{\tau^2}+\frac{x}{\sigma^2}\Big) = \frac{\frac{m}{\tau^2}+\frac{x}{\sigma^2}}{\frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{1}{\sigma^2}} = \frac{\sigma^2 m + \tau^2 x}{\sigma^2 + \tau^2}. $$ By B.5.4 the Bayes estimate is \(m^{*}\), and the posterior variance \(v\) prices the remaining uncertainty.

Example B.8.3
Numbers: one loud measurement

Prior: \(\theta\sim N(100, 15^2)\) (say, an aptitude score known to average 100 with spread 15 in the population the subject comes from). Instrument: \(\sigma=10\). Observation: \(x=130\)—a strikingly high reading. Posterior, by B.8.2, in exact fractions: $$ \frac1v \;=\; \frac{1}{225}+\frac{1}{100} \;=\; \frac{4}{900}+\frac{9}{900} \;=\; \frac{13}{900} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad v \;=\; \frac{900}{13} \;\approx\; 69.2, \quad \sqrt{v} \;=\; \frac{30}{\sqrt{13}} \;\approx\; 8.3; $$ $$ m^{*} \;=\; v\Big( \frac{100}{225} + \frac{130}{100} \Big) \;=\; \frac{900}{13}\Big( \frac{4}{9} + \frac{13}{10} \Big) \;=\; \frac{900}{13}\cdot\frac{40 + 117}{90} \;=\; \frac{10\cdot 157}{13} \;=\; \frac{1570}{13} \;\approx\; 120.8. $$ The Bayes estimate is about \(120.8\): well above the prior's 100 (the measurement was loud and the instrument decent), but pulled down meaningfully from the raw reading 130 (a single reading, from a population where 130 is rare, is partly signal and partly luck). The posterior spread \(8.3\) is smaller than both the prior's 15 and the instrument's 10: two imperfect sources of information, combined, know more than either alone.

Remark B.8.5
The precision reading, and shrinkage

State B.8.2's formulas in terms of precision \(=\) 1/variance (how much a source of information knows): $$ \underbrace{\frac1v}_{\text{posterior precision}} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{\tau^2}}_{\text{prior precision}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sigma^2}}_{\text{data precision}}, \qquad m^{*} \;=\; \frac{\frac{1}{\tau^2}}{\frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{1}{\sigma^2}}\; m \;+\; \frac{\frac{1}{\sigma^2}}{\frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{1}{\sigma^2}}\; x: $$ precisions add, and the posterior mean weighs each guess by its share of the total precision—the continuous twin of B.7.3's observation-counting. In the numbers of B.8.3 the weights are \(\frac{4}{13}\) on the prior and \(\frac{9}{13}\) on the data: \(m^*=\frac{4}{13}\cdot100+\frac{9}{13}\cdot130=\frac{400+1170}{13}=\frac{1570}{13}\), as before. Equivalently, $$ m^{*} \;=\; m + \frac{\tau^2}{\sigma^2+\tau^2}\,(x - m) \;=\; 100 + \frac{225}{325}\,(130-100) \;=\; 100 + \frac{9}{13}\cdot30 \;\approx\; 120.8: $$ the estimate starts at the prior mean and covers only the fraction \(\tau^2/(\sigma^2+\tau^2)\) of the distance to the data. Bayesian estimates shrink noisy observations toward what was already believed—gently when the data are precise, firmly when they are not. This one word, shrinkage, is the modern doorway from this handout into a large part of contemporary statistics and machine learning.

B.9A Third Instrument: Counts—and the One Formula All Three Share

A fraction (§B.7), a measurement (§B.8)—the third workhorse setting is a count: arrivals per hour, defects per batch, calls per shift. The same three-step recipe runs once more, and then this section collects the reward for having watched it three times: all three answers are instances of one formula, a linear combination of the prior's guess and the data's average, with weights that say how much each side knows.

Definition B.9.1
The Poisson distribution

A count \(X\) (values \(0, 1, 2, \dots\), no upper limit) has the Poisson(\(\theta\)) distribution, \(\theta>0\), if $$ P(X = x) \;=\; \frac{e^{-\theta}\,\theta^{x}}{x!}, \qquad x = 0, 1, 2, \dots $$ It is the standard model for the number of events in a window of time when events occur independently at a steady underlying rate \(\theta\). Its mean is that rate: from the definition of expectation (B.2.3), dropping the \(x=0\) term (it contributes nothing) and cancelling one factor of \(x\) into the factorial, $$ \E\{X\} \;=\; \sum_{x=1}^{\infty} x\,\frac{e^{-\theta}\theta^{x}}{x!} \;=\; e^{-\theta}\,\theta \sum_{x=1}^{\infty} \frac{\theta^{\,x-1}}{(x-1)!} \;=\; e^{-\theta}\,\theta\, e^{\theta} \;=\; \theta, $$ the last sum being the exponential series \(\sum_{k\ge0}\theta^k/k! = e^{\theta}\) with \(k=x-1\). (The same maneuver applied to \(\E\{X(X-1)\}\) gives \(\theta^2\), whence \(\Var(X)=\theta\) as well: for a Poisson count, mean and variance are the same number.)

Definition B.9.2
The Gamma distribution

For parameters \(a>0\) (shape) and \(b>0\) (rate), the Gamma(\(a,b\)) distribution is the continuous distribution on \((0,\infty)\) with density $$ \pi(\theta) \;=\; \frac{b^{\,a}\,\theta^{\,a-1}\,e^{-b\theta}}{\Gamma(a)}, \qquad \theta > 0. $$ The constant is right because substituting \(u = b\theta\) (so \(\theta = u/b\), \(d\theta = du/b\)) turns the total mass into the Gamma function itself: $$ \int_0^{\infty} \theta^{\,a-1} e^{-b\theta}\, d\theta \;=\; \int_0^{\infty} \Big(\frac{u}{b}\Big)^{\!a-1} e^{-u}\, \frac{du}{b} \;=\; \frac{1}{b^{\,a}} \int_0^{\infty} u^{\,a-1} e^{-u}\, du \;=\; \frac{\Gamma(a)}{b^{\,a}}. $$ Its mean comes from the kernel trick of B.6.3, verbatim: multiplying the density by \(\theta\) raises the shape by one, and the resulting integral is the Gamma(\(a+1,b\)) normalizer, $$ \E\{\theta\} \;=\; \frac{b^{\,a}}{\Gamma(a)} \int_0^{\infty} \theta^{\,(a+1)-1} e^{-b\theta}\, d\theta \;=\; \frac{b^{\,a}}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+1)}{b^{\,a+1}} \;=\; \frac{a}{b}, $$ using \(\Gamma(a+1)=a\,\Gamma(a)\) (B.6.1). Read \((a,b)\) the way B.7.3 taught: \(a\) behaves like a count of phantom events observed over \(b\) phantom windows, and the prior's best guess at the rate is their ratio \(a/b\).

Example B.9.3
The Poisson–gamma update, by kernel-matching

Watch \(n\) windows: counts \(X_1,\dots,X_n\), independent Poisson(\(\theta\)) given the rate, with total \(s = x_1+\cdots+x_n\). The likelihood multiplies (independence), and everything free of \(\theta\) falls away: $$ f(x_1,\dots,x_n \mid \theta) \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{e^{-\theta}\theta^{x_i}}{x_i!} \;\propto\; e^{-n\theta}\,\theta^{\,s}. $$ Take a Gamma(\(a,b\)) prior and multiply (Bayes' theorem in proportional form, B.4.5): $$ \pi(\theta \mid \text{data}) \;\propto\; \underbrace{\theta^{\,a-1} e^{-b\theta}}_{\text{prior}}\;\cdot\; \underbrace{\theta^{\,s} e^{-n\theta}}_{\text{likelihood}} \;=\; \theta^{\,(a+s)-1}\, e^{-(b+n)\theta}: $$ the Gamma(\(a+s,\; b+n\)) kernel—events reinforce the shape, windows reinforce the rate. By B.9.2 the Bayes estimate is $$ \E\{\theta \mid \text{data}\} \;=\; \frac{a+s}{b+n}. $$

Example B.9.4
Numbers: arrivals at a clinic

Historical experience suggests a clinic sees about 3 arrivals per hour, with the confidence of roughly two hours' worth of watching: prior Gamma(6, 2) (mean \(6/2=3\); worth \(b=2\) phantom hours carrying \(a=6\) phantom arrivals). Now watch \(n=5\) hours and count \(4, 2, 5, 3, 3\) arrivals: total \(s=17\), average \(\bar x = 17/5 = 3.4\). The posterior is Gamma(\(6+17,\; 2+5\)) \(=\) Gamma(23, 7), and the Bayes estimate is $$ \E\{\theta \mid \text{data}\} \;=\; \frac{23}{7} \;\approx\; 3.29, \qquad\text{with posterior sd } \frac{\sqrt{23}}{7} \approx 0.69 $$ (the Gamma(\(a,b\)) variance is \(a/b^2\), by one more round of the kernel trick). And notice the anatomy, one more time: $$ \frac{23}{7} \;=\; \frac{2\cdot 3 \;+\; 5\cdot 3.4}{2+5} \;=\; \frac{b\cdot\dfrac{a}{b} \;+\; n\cdot\bar x}{b+n}: $$ prior guess 3 weighted by its 2 phantom hours, data average 3.4 weighted by the 5 real ones.

Proposition B.9.5
Three pairs, one formula: the Bayes estimate is a linear combination

In each of the handout's three conjugate pairs, the Bayes estimate is the same linear combination—a weighted average of the prior mean \(m_0\) and the sample mean \(\bar x\), with weights set by the prior's worth \(n_0\) (measured in observations) against the sample size \(n\): $$ \E\{\theta \mid \text{data}\} \;=\; \frac{n_0}{\,n_0+n\,}\; m_0 \;+\; \frac{n}{\,n_0+n\,}\; \bar x, $$ with $$ \begin{array}{llll} \text{pair} & \text{prior} & m_0 & n_0 \\ \hline \text{binomial–beta (§B.7)} & \text{Beta}(\alpha,\beta) & \alpha/(\alpha+\beta) & \alpha+\beta \\ \text{normal–normal (§B.8)} & N(m,\tau^2) & m & \sigma^2/\tau^2 \\ \text{Poisson–gamma (§B.9)} & \text{Gamma}(a,b) & a/b & b \end{array} $$

Proof B.9.5
Proof of the One Formula by Assembling the Three Cases.
  1. For the binomial–beta pair, the sample mean of the \(n\) cure indicators is the observed rate, \(\bar x = x/n\), and B.7.3 already wrote the estimate in exactly the displayed form: $$ \frac{\alpha+x}{\alpha+\beta+n} \;=\; \frac{(\alpha+\beta)\cdot\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} + n\cdot\frac{x}{n}} {(\alpha+\beta)+n}, $$ which is the formula with \(m_0=\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)\) and \(n_0=\alpha+\beta\).
  2. For the Poisson–gamma pair the identification is identical, from B.9.4's anatomy display with general symbols: \(\dfrac{a+s}{b+n}=\dfrac{b\cdot\frac{a}{b}+n\cdot\bar x}{b+n}\) (since \(s=n\bar x\)), so \(m_0=a/b\) and \(n_0=b\).
  3. For the normal–normal pair, \(n\) measurements enter through \(\bar x\) with noise variance \(\sigma^2/n\) (B.8.2), so the posterior-mean weights of B.8.5 are the precision shares $$ \E\{\theta\mid\text{data}\} \;=\; \frac{\frac{1}{\tau^2}}{\frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{n}{\sigma^2}}\; m \;+\; \frac{\frac{n}{\sigma^2}}{\frac{1}{\tau^2}+\frac{n}{\sigma^2}}\; \bar x. $$ Multiply the top and bottom of both fractions by \(\sigma^2\): $$ \E\{\theta\mid\text{data}\} \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2/\tau^2}{\,\sigma^2/\tau^2+n\,}\; m \;+\; \frac{n}{\,\sigma^2/\tau^2+n\,}\; \bar x, $$ the formula with \(m_0=m\) and \(n_0=\sigma^2/\tau^2\). The worth \(n_0\) has a concrete reading: it is the number of real measurements whose combined precision, \(n_0/\sigma^2\), exactly matches the prior's precision \(1/\tau^2\).
Uses B.7.3, B.8.2, B.8.5, B.9.3.
Remark B.9.6
Reading the one formula

The formula is a convex linear combination: the weights are nonnegative and sum to 1, so the Bayes estimate always sits between the prior's guess and the data's average, never outside them. The weight on the data, \(n/(n_0+n)\), climbs from 0 (no data: report \(m_0\), as in B.7.5) toward 1 (the data drown the phantoms)—one dial, turned by sample size. This weighting has an old name in actuarial work, where the formula is used to blend a new client's short claims record with the book rate for their class: the data weight \(n/(n_0+n)\) is called the credibility factor. And the pattern is not a coincidence of three lucky examples: for the standard families of models (the exponential families, which include all three pairs here) paired with their conjugate priors, the posterior mean is always linear in the sample mean—and a theorem of Diaconis and Ylvisaker (1979) runs the implication backward: within those families, linearity of the posterior mean is exactly what characterizes conjugate priors. Proving that is beyond this handout; recognizing the formula when it appears—in credibility pricing, in shrinkage estimators, in empirical-Bayes methods—is the skill the three examples were building.

B.10What to Make of the Prior

Remark B.10.1
The estimate depends on the prior—as it must

Rerun the trial of B.7.2 under a different premise. Suppose historical experience with similar treatments made low cure rates plausible—say a Beta(2,6) prior, mean \(2/8=0.25\), worth \(\alpha+\beta=8\) phantom observations. By B.7.3 the posterior after 7 cures in 10 is Beta(\(2+7,\,6+3\)) \(=\) Beta(9,9), with mean $$ \frac{2+7}{2+6+10} \;=\; \frac{9}{18} \;=\; 0.500, $$ against \(0.667\) under the flat prior and \(0.700\) raw. Ten patients cannot overrule eight phantoms. This is not a defect; it is the premise doing exactly what it claimed: with little data, background knowledge should matter, and the machinery is transparent about how much. But it lays an obligation on the analyst—the prior is part of the argument, must be stated, and must be defensible on grounds outside the data being analyzed. Estimating the prior's location from the same data it will then shrink is circular, and the circle does not close.8

Remark B.10.2
The data win in the end

The dependence on the prior is a small-sample phenomenon. Compare the Bayes estimate with the observed rate at stage \(n\) by putting them over a common denominator: $$ \frac{\alpha+x}{\alpha+\beta+n} - \frac{x}{n} \;=\; \frac{n(\alpha+x) - x(\alpha+\beta+n)}{n\,(\alpha+\beta+n)} \;=\; \frac{\alpha n - x(\alpha+\beta)}{n\,(\alpha+\beta+n)} \;=\; \frac{\alpha - (\alpha+\beta)\,\frac{x}{n}}{\alpha+\beta+n}, $$ the middle step cancelling the \(xn\) terms. Since the rate \(x/n\) lies in \([0,1]\), the numerator can be no larger in size than \(\max(\alpha,\beta)\)—a fixed number—while the denominator grows without bound. So the gap between the Bayes estimate and the observed rate shrinks to zero as data accumulate, whatever the data do: every fixed Beta prior's estimate is eventually indistinguishable from the raw rate—the phantoms are outvoted. Priors differing in judgment but agreed on possibilities are eventually reconciled by evidence; where the prior genuinely matters is exactly where matters are genuinely uncertain: small samples.

Three priors meet the same evidence

Remark B.10.3
Two schools, one course

A statistician who declines to put a distribution on \(\theta\)—who insists the cure rate is a fixed fact, not a random variable—works instead with the risk curves of B.3.4 and principles for comparing them (ruling out estimators beaten everywhere; guarding the worst case). That is the frequentist school, and it is where the course notes spend §1.5. The Bayesian school, this handout's subject, buys a complete and unique answer at the price of a prior. The two are not enemies so much as different contracts with the reader about what may be assumed—and they meet constantly: the course's Lecture 1 appendix shows a Bayesian-derived rule, \((x+1)/(n+2)\), holding its own in a purely frequentist risk comparison (1.A.4, 1.A.5). A working statistician reads both languages.

B.11The Vocabulary, and Where to Go Next

Concept B.11.1
The names of everything you just did

For searching, reading, and talking to statisticians—the standard names, each pointing at its object in this handout:

• Prior distribution (B.4.1); likelihood (B.4.5); posterior distribution (B.4.4); Bayes' theorem (B.4.4); Bayesian updating (the whole §B.4 pipeline).
• Bayes risk (B.5.3); Bayes estimator or Bayes rule (B.5.4)—beware that “Bayes' rule” also names the updating theorem; context decides. The posterior-mean result is often quoted as “the Bayes estimator under squared-error (quadratic) loss is the posterior mean.”
• Conjugate prior: a family, like Beta for the binomial (§B.7) or normal for the normal (§B.8), whose posteriors stay in the family—the kernel trick succeeds by design. Shrinkage (B.8.5); Laplace's rule of succession (B.7.5).
• In engineering literature the §B.5 mathematics runs under minimum mean squared error (MMSE) estimation: the conditional expectation as the MMSE predictor.
• One report this handout did not build: an interval from the posterior (e.g., the central 95% of Beta(8,4)), called a credible interval—the Bayesian counterpart of the confidence sets in the course notes, §2.7.
