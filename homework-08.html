Exercise 9 · Best weights

Weight by precision

Three independent measurements of the same mean, with different variances. The minimum-variance unbiased combination \( Z=\sum a_iX_i \), \( \sum a_i=1 \), weights each observation by its reciprocal variance: \( a_i = \sigma_i^{-2}\big/\sum_j\sigma_j^{-2} \). Steer the three variances and watch the weights and the payoff move.

optimal weights \(a_i\) equal weights \(1/3\)

Noisy observations are not discarded—every measurement gets some weight—but precise ones dominate. The optimal variance \( \big(\sum_j\sigma_j^{-2}\big)^{-1} \) says precisions add, the same arithmetic that set the posterior weights in Assignment 7's normal problem.

Exercise 29 · The best multiple of s²

What unbiasedness costs

The known-mean variance estimator \(s^2\) is unbiased for \(\sigma^2\) (Exercise 14). But among all multiples \(ks^2\), the expected squared error \( E(ks^2-\sigma^2)^2 = \sigma^4\big[k^2(n+2)/n - 2k + 1\big] \) is minimized not at \(k=1\) but at \( k = n/(n+2) \): shrinking beats unbiased, at every \(\sigma^2\) and every \(n\). Slide \(n\) and hover the curve.

\(E(ks^2-\sigma^2)^2/\sigma^4\) minimizer \(k=n/(n+2)\) unbiased \(k=1\)

As \(n\) grows the two dots merge and the argument fades—the unbiasedness penalty is a small-sample phenomenon. At small \(n\) it is not small: at \(n=2\) the shrunken estimator's expected squared error is half the unbiased one's.

Exercise 61 · A ledger settled in any order

Sequential updating equals batch updating

Uniform prior; a first experiment with \(s\) successes in \(m\) trials; a second with \(S\) in \(n\), using the first posterior as the new prior. The sequential posterior Beta\((1+s+S,\,1+m+n-s-S)\) is drawn dashed over the batch posterior (all \(m+n\) trials against the uniform prior at once), drawn solid. They are the same curve at every setting of the sliders—that is the exercise's answer.

batch: uniform prior, all m+n trials sequential: first posterior as second prior intermediate posterior after experiment 1

Multiplication does not care how the factors are grouped: posterior ∝ prior × likelihood₁ × likelihood₂ either way. The intermediate posterior (gray) is a complete record of the first experiment—yesterday's posterior serves as today's prior without loss.
