Problem 3 · Distribution

One distribution, two portraits

The distribution of a random variable is the assignment \(A \mapsto P(X\in A)\); in practice it is handled through the pmf and reconstructed by the cdf, whose jump at \(x\) is exactly \(P(X=x)\). Here \(X\sim\text{Binomial}(n,p)\)—the arm total of the course's clinical trial. Drag \(n\) and \(p\) and watch both portraits carry the same information.

pmf P(X = k)
cdf F(x) = P(X ≤ x)

The expectation \( \E\{X\}=np \) is the balancing point of the left panel—often a value the variable never takes—and \( \operatorname{Var}(X)=np(1-p) \) is largest at \(p=\tfrac12\), where each trial is least predictable. Hover the stems to read probabilities.

Problem 10 · Covariance

The sign of covariance, in data

Covariance asks one question of a cloud of points: when \(X\) lands above its mean, does \(Y\) tend to land above its own? The slider sets the population correlation \(\rho\) of a standardized pair; the cloud (a fixed sample of 140 draws) and the line \(y=\rho x\) respond. The sample covariance printed below is computed from the dots you see.

sample (140 fixed draws) population line \(y=\rho x\)

Positive \(\rho\): misses agree in direction. Negative: they oppose. Near zero: no linear pattern. The magnitude of a covariance carries the two variables' units, which is why the standardized version—the correlation—is what travels between problems.

Problem 12 · Independence and covariance

Dependent but uncorrelated

Independence forces zero covariance; the converse fails. The standard counterexample: \(X\) uniform on \(\{-1,0,1\}\) and \(Y=X^2\). Then \( \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\E\{X^3\}-\E\{X\}\E\{X^2\}=0 \), yet \(Y\) is a function of \(X\). Draw samples and watch the running sample covariance settle on zero while the dependence stays total—every point sits on the parabola.

Covariance registers only linear association, and this dependence is pure curvature: \(P(Y=1\mid X=1)=1\) while \(P(Y=1)=\tfrac23\). Knowing \(X\) determines \(Y\) exactly—no independence—yet the best-fitting line through the cloud is flat.
